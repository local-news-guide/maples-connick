Georgia Law

Modified Comparative Fault: The 50% Bar

Georgia follows a modified comparative fault system with a 50% bar. This places Georgia between Louisiana's plaintiff-friendly pure comparative fault and Alabama's harsh contributory negligence standard.

Under Georgia's fault rules (O.C.G.A. § 51-12-33), an injured person can recover damages as long as they are less than 50% at fault for the accident. If a jury determines the plaintiff is 50% or more at fault, recovery is completely barred. If the plaintiff is 49% at fault, they can still recover, but the award is reduced by their percentage of fault.

This creates a critical threshold that both sides fight over. Defense attorneys push to attribute at least 50% fault to the plaintiff, knowing that threshold eliminates the claim entirely. Plaintiffs need attorneys who can keep the fault allocation below that line through effective evidence presentation and witness testimony.

Georgia's statute of limitations for personal injury is two years from the date of injury (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-33). Medical malpractice claims are also subject to a two-year statute of limitations but must comply with Georgia's expert affidavit requirement — an affidavit from a qualified medical expert must accompany the complaint at filing.

For nursing home negligence, Georgia law imposes specific duties on long-term care facilities through both state regulations and federal standards. Georgia courts have recognized claims for negligence, breach of fiduciary duty, and violations of residents' rights. Punitive damages may be available in cases of willful misconduct or reckless disregard for resident safety.

Georgia also has a $350,000 cap on noneconomic damages in medical malpractice cases (though this cap was struck down by the Georgia Supreme Court in 2010 in Atlanta Oculoplastic Surgery, P.C. v. Nestlehutt, the legal landscape continues to evolve). Understanding the current state of damage caps and how they affect case valuation is essential for Georgia injury practice.

Know the Difference

Three States, Three Fault Systems

Louisiana

Pure Comparative Fault. You can recover even if you are mostly at fault. Recovery is reduced by your percentage of fault. 1-year prescriptive period.

Alabama

Contributory Negligence. Any fault by the plaintiff bars all recovery. The most demanding standard in the country. 2-year statute of limitations.

Georgia

Modified Comparative Fault (50% bar). You can recover if you are less than 50% at fault. At 50% or above, recovery is barred. 2-year statute of limitations.
