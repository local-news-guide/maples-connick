Permanent Residence

Your Path to a Green Card

Obtaining lawful permanent resident status — a green card — is one of the most important steps in the immigration process. A green card allows you to live and work permanently in the United States, travel freely, and eventually apply for U.S. citizenship. At Maples & Connick, we handle both family-based and employment-based green card cases across Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia.

Family-based green cards are available for immediate relatives of U.S. citizens (spouses, parents, unmarried children under 21) and for other family members through preference categories. Employment-based green cards are available through five preference categories based on the applicant's qualifications, from priority workers (EB-1) to investors (EB-5).

The green card process involves multiple stages: filing the initial petition (I-130 for family, I-140 for employment), waiting for a visa number to become available (based on preference category and country of origin), and completing the final step through either adjustment of status (I-485, for applicants in the U.S.) or consular processing (for applicants abroad).

We also handle conditional green card removal (I-751 for spouses married less than two years), green card renewal (I-90), and issues related to maintaining permanent resident status during extended travel abroad. Call 504-269-3870 for a consultation.

Cases We Handle

Types of Green Card Cases

Family-Based Green Cards

Petitions for spouses, parents, children, and siblings of U.S. citizens and permanent residents. Immediate relative petitions have no annual cap and process faster than preference categories.

Employment-Based Green Cards

EB-1 through EB-5 preference categories for priority workers, professionals with advanced degrees, skilled workers, special immigrants, and investors.

Adjustment of Status

Filing I-485 to adjust from a nonimmigrant visa to permanent resident status while remaining in the United States, including work authorization and advance parole during the process.

Removing Conditions (I-751)

Conditional residents who received green cards through marriage must file to remove conditions within the 90-day window before expiration. We handle both joint filings and waivers for divorced applicants.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to get a green card?

It depends on the category and your country of birth. Immediate relatives of U.S. citizens can obtain green cards in 12-18 months. Other family and employment preference categories may involve wait times of several years due to annual visa quotas and per-country limits.

Can I work while waiting for my green card?

If you file for adjustment of status (I-485) while in the U.S., you can apply for an Employment Authorization Document (EAD) that allows you to work while your green card is pending. You can also apply for advance parole to travel abroad without abandoning your application.

What is the difference between adjustment of status and consular processing?

Adjustment of status (I-485) allows you to complete the green card process within the U.S. Consular processing requires you to attend an interview at a U.S. embassy or consulate abroad. The best option depends on your current immigration status, location, and travel needs.
