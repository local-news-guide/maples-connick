Common Questions

Immigration FAQ

How long does the green card process take?

Green card processing times vary significantly depending on the category, your country of origin, and current USCIS backlogs. Family-based petitions for immediate relatives of U.S. citizens can take 12 to 24 months, while other family preference categories and employment-based applications may take several years. Our attorneys can evaluate your specific situation and provide a realistic timeline.

What should I do if I receive a Notice to Appear in immigration court?

A Notice to Appear (NTA) initiates removal proceedings and requires immediate legal attention. Do not ignore it. Contact an immigration attorney right away. You have the right to legal representation in immigration court, and having an experienced lawyer can make the difference between remaining in the United States and deportation. Call us at 504-269-3870 for an urgent consultation.

Can I apply for asylum if I am already in the United States?

Yes. You may apply for asylum affirmatively by filing Form I-589 with USCIS, generally within one year of your arrival in the United States. If you are in removal proceedings, you can apply for asylum defensively before an immigration judge. Exceptions to the one-year filing deadline exist for changed or extraordinary circumstances. An attorney can assess whether you qualify.

Do you offer consultations in Spanish or Portuguese?

Yes. Attorney Chanel Payne is fluent in English, Spanish, and Portuguese. She conducts consultations, prepares documents, and represents clients in all three languages. You do not need to bring a translator to your appointment.

What is the difference between a visa and a green card?

A visa grants temporary permission to enter or remain in the United States for a specific purpose, such as work, study, or tourism. A green card (lawful permanent resident card) grants the right to live and work permanently in the United States. Green card holders can eventually apply for U.S. citizenship. Our attorneys help clients with both temporary visa applications and the path to permanent residency.
