Asylum Protection

A Lifeline for Those Fleeing Persecution

Asylum is a form of protection that allows individuals who have suffered persecution — or who have a well-founded fear of future persecution — to remain in the United States. Persecution must be based on one of five protected grounds: race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a particular social group.

The asylum process in the United States follows two paths. Affirmative asylum is filed by individuals who are physically present in the U.S. and not in removal proceedings — they submit an I-589 application to USCIS and attend an interview with an asylum officer. Defensive asylum is raised as a defense against deportation in immigration court before an immigration judge.

A critical requirement is the one-year filing deadline: asylum applications must generally be filed within one year of arriving in the United States. There are limited exceptions for changed circumstances or extraordinary circumstances, but missing this deadline can bar an otherwise meritorious claim.

Cases We Handle

Types of Asylum Cases

Affirmative Asylum

Applications filed with USCIS by individuals present in the U.S. who are not in removal proceedings. Includes preparation for the asylum office interview and compilation of supporting evidence.

Defensive Asylum

Asylum claims raised in immigration court as a defense against deportation. Includes presenting testimony, country condition evidence, and expert witnesses before an immigration judge.

Credible Fear Interviews

Individuals apprehended at or near the border must pass a credible fear interview to establish eligibility for asylum proceedings. We prepare clients for these critical screening interviews.

Withholding of Removal & CAT

Alternative forms of protection for individuals who face persecution or torture but may not qualify for asylum (such as those who missed the one-year deadline). These protections have higher evidentiary standards but no filing deadline.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the one-year filing deadline for asylum?

You must generally file your asylum application (I-589) within one year of your last arrival in the United States. Exceptions exist for changed circumstances (new conditions in your home country or changed personal circumstances) and extraordinary circumstances (serious illness, legal disability, or ineffective assistance of prior counsel). Missing this deadline without an applicable exception bars your asylum claim.

What evidence do I need for an asylum case?

Key evidence includes a detailed personal declaration describing the persecution you experienced or fear, country condition reports (from the State Department, human rights organizations, and news sources), corroborating documents (medical records, police reports, photographs), and sometimes expert witness testimony on country conditions. We help compile all evidence into a comprehensive package.

Can my family members be included in my asylum application?

Yes. Your spouse and unmarried children under 21 who are in the U.S. can be included as derivative applicants on your asylum application. If your asylum is granted, they receive the same protection. Family members abroad may be able to follow to join after your case is approved.
