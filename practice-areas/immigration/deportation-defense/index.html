Deportation Defense

Fighting to Keep You in the United States

Receiving a Notice to Appear (NTA) from Immigration and Customs Enforcement is one of the most frightening experiences an immigrant can face. An NTA initiates removal proceedings in immigration court and starts a process that can result in deportation from the United States — separation from your family, your job, and the life you have built here.

At Maples & Connick, we represent individuals in removal proceedings before immigration judges across Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia. Our immigration attorney Chanel Payne handles every stage of deportation defense — from initial bond hearings to secure release from ICE detention, through merits hearings where we present defenses against removal.

Defenses against deportation may include cancellation of removal (for long-term residents who can demonstrate exceptional hardship to qualifying relatives), asylum or withholding of removal, adjustment of status based on an approved family or employment petition, prosecutorial discretion requests, and relief under the Convention Against Torture (CAT).

Time is critical in deportation defense. If you or a family member has been detained by ICE or received a Notice to Appear, contact us immediately at 504-269-3870. We offer consultations in English, Spanish, and Portuguese.

Cases We Handle

Types of Deportation Defense Cases

Removal Proceedings

Representation before immigration judges in removal hearings, including presenting defenses, cross-examining government witnesses, and filing applications for relief from removal.

Bond Hearings

Securing release from ICE detention through bond hearings before immigration judges. We demonstrate that clients are not a flight risk or danger to the community.

Cancellation of Removal

Relief available to certain long-term residents who can demonstrate continuous physical presence, good moral character, and that deportation would cause exceptional and extremely unusual hardship to qualifying relatives.

ICE Detention

Advocating for detained individuals, challenging prolonged detention, and pursuing release through bond, parole, or habeas corpus petitions when detention is unjustified.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I do if ICE comes to my door?

You have the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney. You do not have to open the door unless ICE has a judicial warrant (signed by a judge, not an ICE agent). Do not sign any documents without an attorney present. Contact a lawyer immediately.

Can I be deported if I have U.S. citizen children?

Having U.S. citizen children does not automatically prevent deportation, but it may provide a basis for cancellation of removal if you can demonstrate that your removal would cause "exceptional and extremely unusual hardship" to your citizen children. This is a high legal standard, but it is a viable defense in many cases.

How long can ICE detain me?

ICE detention should not be indefinite. After a final order of removal, ICE generally has 90 days to effectuate deportation. If removal is not reasonably foreseeable, continued detention may be challenged. During removal proceedings, you may be eligible for bond or release on your own recognizance.
