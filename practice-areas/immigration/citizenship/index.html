Citizenship

Your Path to U.S. Citizenship

Naturalization — the process of becoming a U.S. citizen — grants permanent residents the right to vote, hold public office, obtain a U.S. passport, sponsor additional family members for immigration, and enjoy the full protections of U.S. citizenship. At Maples & Connick, we guide lawful permanent residents through the naturalization process from application through the oath ceremony.

To qualify for naturalization, most applicants must be at least 18 years old, have held a green card for at least five years (three years if married to a U.S. citizen), demonstrate continuous residence and physical presence in the United States, show good moral character, pass an English language test, and pass a civics test on U.S. history and government.

We help clients prepare complete N-400 applications, compile required documentation, address potential issues (prior arrests, tax problems, extended travel abroad), prepare for the naturalization interview, and study for the English and civics examinations. For clients with disabilities or medical conditions, we assist with disability waivers (N-648) when applicable.

Our offices in New Orleans, Mobile, and Atlanta serve permanent residents across the Gulf South and Southeast. Call 504-269-3870 to discuss your eligibility for U.S. citizenship.

Cases We Handle

Types of Citizenship Cases

Standard Naturalization

N-400 applications for permanent residents who have held green cards for five or more years and meet all eligibility requirements including continuous residence, physical presence, and good moral character.

Marriage-Based (3-Year Rule)

Permanent residents married to U.S. citizens may apply after three years of permanent residence (instead of five) if they have been living in marital union with their citizen spouse throughout that period.

Naturalization Interview Prep

Comprehensive preparation for the USCIS naturalization interview, including practice with English and civics test questions, document review, and guidance on answering background questions.

Derived & Acquired Citizenship

Some individuals may already be U.S. citizens through their parents without realizing it. We analyze family immigration histories to determine if citizenship was acquired at birth or derived through a parent's naturalization.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the naturalization process take?

From filing the N-400 to taking the oath of citizenship, the process typically takes 8-14 months, depending on your USCIS field office. Processing times vary by location. We monitor your case and follow up with USCIS if processing exceeds normal timelines.

Can I become a citizen if I have a criminal record?

A criminal record does not automatically disqualify you from naturalization, but certain convictions can. Aggravated felonies permanently bar naturalization. Other offenses may affect the "good moral character" requirement depending on the nature of the offense and when it occurred. We assess each client's criminal history individually before filing.

Do I have to give up my other citizenship?

The United States generally does not require you to formally renounce your prior citizenship upon naturalization, though you will take an oath of allegiance to the United States. Whether you can maintain dual citizenship depends on the laws of your country of origin. We advise clients on the implications of naturalization for their specific situation.
