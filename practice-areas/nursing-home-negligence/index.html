Protecting the Vulnerable

Holding Nursing Homes & Healthcare Providers Accountable

Families trust nursing homes and medical facilities to provide safe, competent care for their most vulnerable loved ones. When that trust is broken through neglect, abuse, or substandard medical treatment, the consequences can be devastating and sometimes fatal. Maples & Connick represents families across Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia who are confronting institutional negligence at its worst.

Nursing home negligence takes many forms. It can be a facility that chronically understaffs its shifts, leaving residents without basic needs met for hours. It can be caregivers who fail to reposition immobile patients, causing severe pressure ulcers. It can be administrators who ignore documented patterns of abuse. In every case, the common thread is a facility that has prioritized cost-cutting or convenience over the safety of the people in its care.

Medical malpractice in institutional settings compounds the problem. Residents who are already vulnerable because of age, illness, or disability are often subjected to medication errors, missed diagnoses, and delayed treatment that would not be tolerated in other healthcare environments. When a healthcare provider deviates from accepted standards of care and a patient is harmed as a result, that provider and the institution that employs them must be held to account.

Know the Warning Signs

Signs of Nursing Home Negligence

If you notice any of the following in a loved one's care facility, it may indicate neglect or abuse. Do not wait to seek legal counsel.

Unexplained Injuries

Bruises, fractures, cuts, or burns that staff cannot explain or that appear repeatedly. These may indicate physical abuse or dangerous understaffing that leaves residents at risk of falls.

Malnutrition & Dehydration

Sudden weight loss, dry skin, cracked lips, or signs of dehydration suggest that residents are not receiving adequate food and water, a basic obligation every facility owes.

Medication Errors

Wrong medications, incorrect dosages, missed doses, or unexplained changes in alertness or behavior. Medication mismanagement is one of the leading causes of preventable harm in nursing homes.

Bedsores & Pressure Ulcers

Pressure ulcers, especially at advanced stages, are strong evidence that staff is failing to reposition immobile residents. Stage III and IV bedsores are painful, dangerous, and almost always preventable.

Emotional Withdrawal

A resident who was once engaged but becomes fearful, withdrawn, anxious, or unusually silent around certain staff members may be experiencing emotional abuse or intimidation.

Unsanitary Conditions

Soiled bedding left unchanged, strong odors, pest infestations, or residents found in dirty clothing all point to systemic neglect at the facility level, not isolated incidents.

Medical Malpractice

When Healthcare Providers Fail Their Patients

Medical malpractice occurs when a doctor, nurse, surgeon, or other provider deviates from the accepted standard of care and causes harm. We handle the full range of medical negligence claims.

Surgical Errors

Wrong-site surgery, retained instruments, nerve damage, and procedures performed without proper consent. Surgical mistakes can cause permanent disability and require additional corrective procedures.

Misdiagnosis & Delayed Diagnosis

A missed cancer diagnosis, a heart attack dismissed as anxiety, an infection left untreated. When a correct diagnosis comes too late, treatment options narrow and outcomes worsen dramatically.

Birth Injuries

Cerebral palsy, Erb's palsy, brain injuries from oxygen deprivation, and other preventable birth injuries caused by failures in prenatal care, labor management, or delivery.

Medication Errors

Prescribing the wrong drug, dangerous drug interactions, incorrect dosages, and pharmacy dispensing mistakes. Medication errors are among the most common and most preventable forms of malpractice.

Anesthesia Errors

Administering too much or too little anesthesia, failing to review patient history for contraindications, or inadequate monitoring during surgery. Anesthesia errors can result in brain damage, paralysis, or death.

Hospital & Institutional Negligence

Dangerous staffing ratios, failure to enforce infection control protocols, inadequate supervision of residents and trainees, and systemic failures that put patients at risk across an entire facility.

Common Questions

Nursing Home & Medical Malpractice FAQ

What are the most common signs of nursing home neglect?

Common signs include unexplained bruises or injuries, sudden weight loss or dehydration, bedsores (pressure ulcers), unsanitary living conditions, emotional withdrawal or unusual fearfulness, and medication errors. If you notice any of these in a loved one, contact an attorney immediately. Early intervention can protect your family member and preserve critical evidence.

How long do I have to file a nursing home negligence lawsuit?

Statutes of limitations vary by state. In Louisiana, you generally have one year from the date of discovery. Alabama allows a two-year window, and Georgia provides two years as well. Because these deadlines are strict and exceptions are limited, you should consult an attorney as soon as you suspect negligence. Missing the deadline can permanently bar your claim.

Can I sue a nursing home if my loved one died from neglect?

Yes. Wrongful death claims can be filed by surviving family members when a nursing home resident dies as a result of negligence or abuse. These cases can seek compensation for medical expenses, funeral costs, loss of companionship, and pain and suffering the resident experienced before death. Each state has specific rules about who can bring a wrongful death claim, so consult an attorney to understand your rights.

What qualifies as medical malpractice?

Medical malpractice occurs when a healthcare provider deviates from the accepted standard of care and that deviation causes injury to the patient. Examples include surgical errors, misdiagnosis or delayed diagnosis, medication mistakes, birth injuries, and anesthesia errors. To pursue a claim, you generally need to demonstrate what the standard of care was, how the provider fell short, and that the deviation directly caused your injury.
