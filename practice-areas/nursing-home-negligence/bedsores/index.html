Pressure Injuries

Bedsores Are Neglect

Pressure ulcers — commonly called bedsores — develop when sustained pressure on the skin reduces blood flow to the tissue. They typically appear on bony prominences: the sacrum (tailbone), heels, hips, elbows, and shoulder blades. In a properly staffed and managed nursing home, pressure ulcers are almost entirely preventable through regular repositioning, proper nutrition, skin assessments, and appropriate support surfaces.

The development of a Stage III or Stage IV pressure ulcer in a nursing home resident is a strong indicator of neglect. Stage III ulcers extend through the full thickness of the skin into underlying fat tissue. Stage IV ulcers penetrate even deeper, exposing muscle, tendon, or bone. At these stages, the wounds are extremely painful, prone to life-threatening infection (including sepsis and osteomyelitis), and may require surgical intervention. Many elderly patients with advanced pressure ulcers do not survive.

Federal nursing home regulations (CMS F-Tag 686) require facilities to ensure that residents who enter without pressure ulcers do not develop them unless clinically unavoidable, and that residents who have existing pressure ulcers receive treatment to promote healing. Facilities must conduct skin assessments, implement Braden Scale risk evaluations, establish turning schedules, provide pressure-relieving mattresses, and ensure adequate nutrition. Failure to meet these requirements constitutes regulatory non-compliance and evidence of negligence.

Cases We Handle

Types of Pressure Injury Cases

Stage III & IV Pressure Ulcers

Full-thickness skin loss extending into fat, muscle, tendon, or bone. These advanced wounds indicate prolonged neglect and failure to implement basic pressure injury prevention protocols.

Sepsis from Infected Wounds

Pressure ulcers that become infected can lead to sepsis — a life-threatening systemic infection. Sepsis from bedsores is a leading cause of death in neglected nursing home residents.

Failure to Reposition

Immobile residents must be repositioned every two hours to prevent pressure buildup. Facilities that fail to maintain turning schedules due to understaffing are directly responsible for resulting pressure injuries.

Inadequate Wound Care

Facilities that fail to properly assess, document, and treat existing pressure ulcers — allowing wounds to worsen from Stage I to Stage IV when proper care could have promoted healing.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Are bedsores always a sign of nursing home neglect?

In the vast majority of cases, yes. CMS regulations recognize that pressure ulcers are preventable with proper care. A facility must demonstrate that the ulcer was "clinically unavoidable" despite all appropriate interventions. In practice, advanced-stage bedsores almost always indicate that the facility failed to reposition the resident, provide adequate nutrition, or conduct proper skin assessments.

How quickly can bedsores develop?

Pressure ulcers can begin forming within hours of sustained pressure, but progression to advanced stages typically takes days to weeks of continued neglect. A Stage I ulcer (reddened skin) can progress to a Stage IV ulcer (exposed bone) in a matter of weeks if the facility fails to intervene. This progression is documented in the medical record and serves as direct evidence of ongoing neglect.

What compensation is available in bedsore cases?

Families can pursue compensation for medical treatment costs, pain and suffering endured by the resident, wrongful death if the pressure ulcer contributed to death, and in cases of egregious neglect, punitive damages designed to punish the facility and deter future misconduct.
