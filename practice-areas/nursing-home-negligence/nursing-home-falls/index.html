Fall Injuries

Falls Are Preventable

Falls are the leading cause of injury and injury-related death among nursing home residents. The CDC reports that approximately 50 to 75 percent of nursing home residents fall each year — roughly twice the rate of community-dwelling older adults. For elderly residents with osteoporosis, impaired mobility, or cognitive decline, a single fall can result in a hip fracture, traumatic brain injury, or death.

The critical fact about nursing home falls is that most of them are preventable. Federal regulations require every nursing facility to assess each resident's fall risk upon admission and develop an individualized care plan to minimize that risk. This includes bed alarms, non-slip footwear, adequate lighting, clear pathways, assistance with transfers, and — above all — sufficient staffing to respond to residents who need help moving safely.

When a nursing home fails to conduct proper fall risk assessments, ignores care plan interventions, operates with dangerously low staffing levels, or fails to maintain safe premises, and a resident falls and is seriously injured, the facility is liable for that negligence.

Cases We Handle

Types of Nursing Home Fall Cases

Hip Fractures

Falls that result in hip fractures, which carry a 20-30% one-year mortality rate in elderly patients. Hip fractures often require surgery and lengthy rehabilitation that many elderly residents never fully recover from.

Head Injuries

Falls resulting in traumatic brain injuries, subdural hematomas, and skull fractures. Residents on blood thinners face especially high risk of fatal brain bleeds from even minor head impacts.

Wheelchair and Transfer Falls

Falls during transfers from beds, wheelchairs, toilets, and showers due to inadequate assistance, faulty equipment, or insufficient staffing. Residents who need two-person transfers are especially vulnerable.

Unattended Falls

Residents who fall while walking unassisted in hallways or attempting to get out of bed without help because no staff member responded to their call light or was available to assist.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Are all nursing home falls considered negligence?

No. Some falls occur despite proper precautions. However, a facility is liable when it fails to assess fall risk, fails to implement care plan interventions, operates with inadequate staffing, or fails to maintain safe premises. The key question is whether the facility took reasonable steps to prevent the fall.

What records should I request after a loved one falls?

Request the incident/accident report, the resident's care plan, fall risk assessment, medication list (some medications increase fall risk), staffing schedules for the shift when the fall occurred, and any surveillance footage. An attorney can subpoena these records if the facility refuses to provide them.

Can a nursing home be liable if my loved one dies after a fall?

Yes. If the fall was caused by the facility's negligence and the injuries contributed to your loved one's death, the family may pursue a wrongful death claim. Hip fractures in elderly residents carry a high mortality rate, and the fall itself may be the proximate cause of death even if death occurs weeks or months later.
