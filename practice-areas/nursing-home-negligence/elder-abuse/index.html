Elder Abuse

Protecting the Most Vulnerable

Elder abuse in nursing homes is a national crisis. According to the National Center on Elder Abuse, approximately one in ten Americans aged 60 and older has experienced some form of abuse. In institutional settings like nursing homes and assisted living facilities, the rate may be even higher because many victims are cognitively impaired, physically dependent, or too frightened to report what is happening to them.

At Maples & Connick, we represent families whose loved ones have suffered physical violence, emotional cruelty, sexual assault, or financial exploitation at the hands of nursing home staff or other residents. These cases often reveal systemic failures — understaffing, inadequate background checks, insufficient training, and a corporate culture that prioritizes profit over resident safety.

We investigate elder abuse claims by obtaining facility records, staffing logs, incident reports, state inspection findings, and surveillance footage when available. We review CMS deficiency reports and state licensing actions to identify patterns of neglect. When the abuse is egregious, Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia all provide for enhanced damages beyond compensatory relief.

If you suspect a loved one is being abused in a nursing home, act immediately. Document any visible injuries with photographs, report the suspected abuse to the state agency (Louisiana Department of Health, Alabama Department of Public Health, or Georgia Healthcare Facility Regulation), and contact Maples & Connick at 504-269-3870 for a free consultation. We handle all cases on contingency.

Cases We Handle

Types of Elder Abuse Cases

Physical Abuse

Hitting, slapping, pushing, improper restraint use, and rough handling by nursing home staff. Signs include unexplained bruises, fractures, welts, and injuries inconsistent with the facility's explanation.

Emotional Abuse

Verbal threats, intimidation, humiliation, isolation, and deliberate ignoring of residents. Emotional abuse causes depression, anxiety, withdrawal, and accelerated cognitive decline.

Sexual Abuse

Any non-consensual sexual contact with a resident, whether by staff or other residents. Facilities have a duty to protect residents from sexual predators, including screening employees and supervising interactions.

Financial Exploitation

Theft of money, valuables, or property. Unauthorized use of financial accounts, forged signatures on legal documents, and coercing residents to change wills or powers of attorney.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the warning signs of elder abuse?

Warning signs include unexplained injuries (bruises, cuts, burns, fractures), sudden behavioral changes (withdrawal, fear, agitation), poor hygiene, weight loss, dehydration, torn or bloody clothing, reluctance to speak in front of staff, and unexplained financial transactions. If you notice any of these signs, document them and report them immediately.

Who is liable when elder abuse occurs in a nursing home?

Liability can extend to the individual abuser, the nursing home facility, the facility's management company, and the corporate owner. Facilities have a legal duty to hire qualified staff, conduct background checks, maintain adequate staffing levels, train employees, and supervise resident care. When they fail these duties and abuse results, the corporate chain is accountable.

Can I file a lawsuit and also report to the state?

Yes, and you should do both. Reporting to the state licensing agency triggers an investigation and can lead to citations, fines, and corrective action plans. A civil lawsuit separately pursues compensation for the harm your loved one suffered. The two processes are independent and complement each other.
