Malnutrition & Dehydration

The Silent Neglect

Malnutrition and dehydration in nursing homes are forms of neglect that kill silently. Unlike a fall or a visible injury, nutritional neglect develops gradually — a resident loses weight week after week, becomes increasingly weak, develops infections that a nourished body could fight off, and ultimately dies from complications that proper care would have prevented. By the time the family notices, the damage may be irreversible.

Federal regulations require nursing homes to provide each resident with a diet that meets their nutritional needs, accommodate dietary restrictions and preferences, provide adequate hydration, and monitor weight and nutritional status through regular MDS assessments. Residents who need assistance eating must receive that assistance. Residents with swallowing difficulties (dysphagia) must receive modified diets and supervised feeding to prevent aspiration.

When a nursing home fails these basic duties — because of understaffing, inadequate training, or cost-cutting on food services — residents suffer. Malnutrition weakens the immune system, delays wound healing (worsening bedsores), causes muscle wasting, and increases the risk of falls. Dehydration causes confusion, urinary tract infections, kidney failure, and cardiac events. Together, they can cause death.

Cases We Handle

Types of Malnutrition & Dehydration Cases

Unexplained Weight Loss

Significant weight loss (5% in one month or 10% in six months) without a documented medical reason. Weight loss at these rates in a nursing home setting is a CMS-recognized indicator of potential neglect.

Dehydration

Failure to provide adequate fluids or monitor hydration status. Dehydration causes confusion, kidney failure, urinary tract infections, and death — and is entirely preventable with basic nursing care.

Aspiration Pneumonia

Residents with swallowing difficulties who are fed inappropriate food textures or left unassisted during meals may aspirate food into their lungs, causing pneumonia. This is a leading cause of death in nursing home residents.

Feeding Assistance Failures

Residents who cannot feed themselves must receive hands-on assistance at every meal. Facilities that rush meals, skip feedings, or assign too many residents to a single aide fail this fundamental duty.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my loved one is malnourished in a nursing home?

Warning signs include unexplained weight loss, loose-fitting clothing, dry or cracked skin, lethargy, confusion, frequent infections, slow wound healing, and complaints of hunger or thirst. Request the facility's weight records and MDS nutritional assessments. If the facility is documenting weight loss but not implementing interventions, that is evidence of neglect.

Can a nursing home be held liable for dehydration?

Yes. Facilities have a legal duty to provide adequate hydration and monitor fluid intake. If a resident becomes dehydrated because the facility failed to offer fluids regularly, failed to assist residents who cannot drink independently, or failed to respond to signs of dehydration, the facility is liable for the resulting harm.

What if the nursing home says my loved one refused to eat?

Facilities cannot simply document "resident refused" and abandon their duty of care. They must investigate why the resident is refusing food (pain, dental problems, medication side effects, depression), involve a physician, try alternative foods and feeding schedules, and document all interventions. Repeated "refused" entries without follow-up is evidence of neglect, not patient autonomy.
