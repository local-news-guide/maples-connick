Fighting for Injured Workers

The Gulf South is an industrial powerhouse — petrochemical plants line the Mississippi River corridor, offshore oil platforms dot the Gulf of Mexico, construction cranes fill the skylines of New Orleans, Mobile, and Atlanta, and warehouses and manufacturing facilities employ hundreds of thousands of workers. When employers and contractors cut corners on safety training, maintenance, and OSHA compliance, workers pay the price with catastrophic injuries: crush injuries, chemical burns, amputations, traumatic brain injuries, and death.

Workers' compensation provides limited benefits for on-the-job injuries, but it does not provide full compensation for pain and suffering, and it caps the benefits available for lost wages and medical treatment. In many workplace injury cases, a third party — a subcontractor, equipment manufacturer, property owner, or another company's employee — bears responsibility for the accident. These third-party claims are not limited by workers' compensation caps and can provide full compensation for all damages, including pain and suffering, future medical care, and diminished earning capacity.

Cases We Handle

Types of Workplace Injury Cases

We represent workers injured in industrial, construction, and oil and gas operations where employer or third-party negligence caused the harm.

Construction Accidents

Falls from height, scaffolding collapses, crane accidents, electrocution, and struck-by incidents on construction sites. Multiple contractors and subcontractors may share liability when safety standards are violated.

Industrial Explosions

Petrochemical plant explosions, refinery fires, chemical releases, and confined-space incidents. These disasters often injure multiple workers simultaneously and involve complex liability chains among owners, operators, and contractors.

Oil & Gas Injuries

Offshore platform accidents, pipeline explosions, drilling rig injuries, and chemical exposure in oil and gas operations. These cases may involve federal maritime law, the Jones Act, or the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act.

Equipment & Machinery Failures

Defective or poorly maintained industrial equipment, power tools, forklifts, and heavy machinery that cause crush injuries, amputations, and electrocution. Claims may lie against the equipment manufacturer, the maintenance provider, or the employer.

Common Questions

Workplace Injury FAQ

Can I sue my employer for a workplace injury?

In most cases, workers' compensation is the exclusive remedy against your direct employer, meaning you cannot sue your employer in a traditional personal injury lawsuit. However, you may have claims against third parties — such as subcontractors, equipment manufacturers, property owners, or other companies whose employees contributed to your injury. These third-party claims are not subject to workers' compensation limits and can provide full compensation for all damages.

What is a third-party workplace injury claim?

A third-party claim is a personal injury lawsuit filed against someone other than your employer who caused or contributed to your workplace injury. For example, if a defective piece of equipment made by a manufacturer caused your injury, you can sue the manufacturer. If a subcontractor's negligence on a construction site led to your accident, you can sue that subcontractor. Third-party claims allow you to recover full damages including pain and suffering, which workers' compensation does not cover.

What should I do if I am injured at work?

Report the injury to your supervisor immediately and seek medical attention. Document the scene and your injuries with photographs if possible. File a workers' compensation claim as required. Then contact an attorney to evaluate whether you have third-party claims that could provide additional compensation beyond what workers' compensation offers. Do not sign any settlements or give recorded statements to insurance adjusters without consulting a lawyer first.

Do offshore workers have different legal rights?

Yes. Workers injured on navigable waters or offshore platforms may be covered under the Jones Act, the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act, or general maritime law — each of which provides different rights and remedies than state workers' compensation. The Jones Act, for example, allows seamen to sue their employers for negligence with the right to a jury trial. Determining which law applies requires a careful analysis of your job duties and work location.
