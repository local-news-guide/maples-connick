Holding Manufacturers Accountable for Dangerous Products

Every product that reaches a consumer should be safe for its intended use. When a product is defectively designed, manufactured with flaws, or sold without adequate warnings, and that defect causes serious injury, every party in the chain of distribution — from the manufacturer to the retailer — can be held legally responsible. Products liability cases involve some of the most powerful corporate defendants in the legal system, and they require attorneys who can match the resources and expertise that these companies bring to their defense.

Louisiana applies a unique products liability framework under the Louisiana Products Liability Act (LPLA), which requires the injured person to prove that the product was unreasonably dangerous due to a construction or composition defect, a design defect, inadequate warning, or failure to conform to an express warranty. Alabama and Georgia follow common law products liability principles that include strict liability, negligence, and breach of warranty theories. Each state's framework affects the type of evidence required, the available defenses, and the damages that can be recovered.

Products liability cases are technically demanding. They require engineering experts to analyze design and manufacturing defects, medical experts to establish causation between the defect and the injury, regulatory specialists to evaluate compliance with industry standards, and often economists to calculate the lifetime cost of the injuries. At Maples & Connick, we have the resources and experience to retain these experts and build cases that can withstand the aggressive defense strategies employed by major manufacturers and their insurance companies.

Cases We Handle

Types of Products Liability Cases

We pursue claims against manufacturers, distributors, and retailers who put dangerous products into the hands of consumers.

Defective Medical Devices

Hip and knee implants, surgical mesh, pacemakers, and other medical devices that fail, malfunction, or cause adverse reactions. These cases often involve FDA recalls, class actions, or multidistrict litigation.

Dangerous Pharmaceuticals

Medications with undisclosed side effects, contaminated drugs, or pharmaceuticals that were rushed to market without adequate testing. Manufacturers have a duty to warn doctors and patients of known risks.

Vehicle Defects

Tire blowouts, airbag failures, seatbelt malfunctions, fuel system defects, and rollover-prone designs that cause or worsen injuries in auto accidents. Claims target the vehicle manufacturer, parts supplier, or both.

Industrial Equipment Failures

Defective machinery, power tools, safety guards, and industrial equipment that cause crush injuries, amputations, and electrocution in workplaces. These cases often overlap with workplace injury and workers' compensation claims.

Common Questions

Products Liability FAQ

What makes a product legally defective?

A product can be defective in three ways: a design defect (the product's design is inherently dangerous), a manufacturing defect (the product was made incorrectly or with flawed materials), or a marketing defect (the product lacked adequate warnings or instructions about its risks). In Louisiana, the LPLA also recognizes liability when a product fails to conform to the manufacturer's express warranty.

Can I sue the store that sold me a defective product?

In many cases, yes. Products liability law allows claims against every party in the chain of distribution — the manufacturer, the distributor, and the retailer. However, the specific theories of liability available against each party depend on state law. In some states, retailers may be strictly liable for selling a defective product even if they had no knowledge of the defect. Our attorneys identify every potentially liable party to maximize the available compensation.

Should I keep the defective product?

Absolutely. Preserve the product exactly as it is — do not repair, alter, discard, or return it. The product itself is the most critical piece of evidence in a products liability case. Take photographs of the product and any visible defects. Keep all packaging, manuals, receipts, and warranty information. If the product was involved in an accident, also preserve any other physical evidence from the incident scene.

What is the statute of limitations for a products liability claim?

Louisiana allows one year from the date the injury is discovered or should have been discovered, with an overall outer limit (prescriptive period) that varies depending on the specific claim. Alabama and Georgia generally allow two years for products liability claims. Some states also impose a statute of repose that bars claims filed more than a certain number of years after the product was first sold, regardless of when the injury occurred.
