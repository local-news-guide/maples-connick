Pursuing Justice for Families Who Have Lost Everything

No amount of money can replace a life. But the legal system provides wrongful death claims as the only mechanism for holding negligent parties accountable and providing financial security to families left behind. When a loved one dies because of a car crash, a trucking accident, a workplace disaster, a defective product, or medical negligence, the family has the right to demand answers — and to demand compensation for everything that was taken from them.

Wrongful death law varies significantly across Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia. Louisiana recognizes both a wrongful death action (brought by surviving family members for their own losses) and a survival action (brought on behalf of the deceased for the pain, suffering, and damages the deceased experienced between the injury and death). Alabama's wrongful death statute is unique in the nation — it focuses primarily on punitive damages rather than compensatory damages. Georgia allows recovery for both the full value of the decedent's life and specific economic losses to the survivors. Understanding these distinctions is critical to maximizing recovery for the family.

The parties who may bring a wrongful death claim also differ by state. In Louisiana, the right to bring a wrongful death claim follows a statutory hierarchy: surviving spouse and children first, then parents, then siblings. Alabama and Georgia have their own rules governing who has standing to file suit. In every case, the family must act within the applicable statute of limitations — which can be as short as one year in Louisiana — or lose the right to pursue the claim entirely.

Cases We Handle

Types of Wrongful Death Claims

We represent families who have lost loved ones to negligence, recklessness, and corporate misconduct.

Motor Vehicle Deaths

Fatal car crashes, truck accidents, motorcycle collisions, and pedestrian deaths caused by negligent, distracted, or impaired drivers. We pursue claims against all at-fault parties and their insurers.

Workplace Fatalities

Industrial explosions, construction collapses, chemical exposure, and other on-the-job deaths. When an employer or third party's negligence causes a worker's death, the family may have claims beyond workers' compensation.

Medical Negligence Deaths

Misdiagnosis, surgical errors, medication mistakes, and hospital negligence that result in a patient's death. These cases require expert medical testimony and detailed review of the standard of care.

Survival Actions

Claims brought on behalf of the deceased person for the pain, suffering, and damages they experienced between the time of the injury and death. In Louisiana, survival actions are separate from wrongful death claims and may be brought by different parties.

Common Questions

Wrongful Death FAQ

Who can file a wrongful death lawsuit?

The answer depends on the state. In Louisiana, the right to file follows a statutory hierarchy: the surviving spouse and children have first priority, followed by parents, then siblings. In Alabama, a personal representative of the estate files the claim. In Georgia, the surviving spouse typically has the right to bring the action, or if there is no spouse, the children or the administrator of the estate may file. Our attorneys will determine who has standing to bring the claim in your specific situation.

What is the difference between a wrongful death claim and a survival action?

A wrongful death claim compensates the surviving family members for their own losses — loss of financial support, loss of love and companionship, funeral expenses, and emotional suffering. A survival action, recognized in Louisiana and other states, compensates the estate for the deceased person's own damages — the pain and suffering they experienced between the injury and death, along with medical expenses and lost wages during that period. These are separate legal claims that may be brought together.

How long do I have to file a wrongful death lawsuit?

Statutes of limitations vary by state. Louisiana generally allows one year from the date of death to file a wrongful death claim. Alabama allows two years, and Georgia also allows two years in most cases. These deadlines are strictly enforced, and missing them will permanently bar the family's right to recover compensation. Contact an attorney as soon as possible to protect your rights.

What damages are available in a wrongful death case?

Recoverable damages depend on state law and the specific facts. They may include loss of the deceased's future income and benefits, loss of love, affection, and companionship, mental anguish of the surviving family, funeral and burial expenses, and medical expenses incurred before death. Alabama's wrongful death statute is uniquely focused on punitive damages. Georgia allows recovery for the full value of the decedent's life. An experienced attorney can explain what damages apply in your case.
