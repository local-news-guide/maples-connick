Catastrophic Injuries

Fighting for Life-Changing Recoveries

Catastrophic injuries are the core of our practice. At Maples & Connick, attorneys Aaron Maples and Byron Forrest dedicate their careers to representing people who have suffered the most devastating injuries — those that permanently alter the course of a life. Spinal cord injuries that cause paralysis. Burns covering large portions of the body. Amputations that eliminate a limb forever. Traumatic brain injuries that change personality, cognition, and the ability to function independently.

What distinguishes catastrophic injury cases from other personal injury claims is the scale of damages involved. A 30-year-old construction worker who becomes a paraplegic in a crane collapse does not just need compensation for past medical bills and lost wages. That individual needs a lifetime of wheelchair equipment, home modifications, attendant care, medical treatment, vocational rehabilitation, and compensation for the life they can no longer live. These future costs regularly reach into the millions of dollars.

Building a catastrophic injury case requires a team of experts: life care planners who project future medical and support needs, economists who calculate lost earning capacity and the present value of future costs, medical specialists who testify about the permanence of injuries, and vocational rehabilitation experts who assess diminished work capacity. At Maples & Connick, we invest in these experts for every catastrophic case because we know that incomplete evidence leads to inadequate compensation.

Cases We Handle

Types of Catastrophic Injury Cases

Spinal Cord Injuries & Paralysis

Complete and incomplete spinal cord injuries resulting in paraplegia, quadriplegia, and loss of sensation. These injuries require lifelong medical care, adaptive equipment, and attendant support.

Severe Burns

Third-degree and fourth-degree burns requiring skin grafts, reconstructive surgery, and long-term wound care. Burn victims often face permanent disfigurement, chronic pain, and psychological trauma.

Amputations

Traumatic amputations or surgical amputations resulting from crush injuries, industrial accidents, or medical negligence. Prosthetic limbs, rehabilitation, and phantom pain management are lifelong needs.

Multiple Trauma

Cases involving multiple severe injuries from a single incident — such as a major car crash or industrial explosion — that compound each other and create complex, multi-system medical needs.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes an injury "catastrophic"?

A catastrophic injury is one that permanently prevents the victim from performing any gainful work or fundamentally alters their ability to live independently. This includes paralysis, severe brain injury, loss of limbs, loss of sight or hearing, and severe burns. The legal significance is that catastrophic injuries involve much larger damages — future medical costs, lifetime lost earnings, and substantial pain and suffering.

How do you calculate damages in a catastrophic injury case?

We retain life care planners, economists, and medical experts to project all future needs and costs. A life care plan details every medical treatment, therapy, piece of equipment, and attendant care the victim will need for their remaining life expectancy. An economist calculates the present value of these future costs plus lost earning capacity. These expert analyses are essential to securing adequate compensation.

Why do catastrophic injury cases take longer to resolve?

Because the stakes are enormous, both sides invest heavily. The defense retains their own medical and economic experts, contests causation and damages, and may take extensive discovery. We also need time for the victim's condition to stabilize so we can accurately project future needs. Settling too early risks undervaluing a claim worth millions of dollars.
