Our Practice

Holding Property Owners Accountable

Slip and fall injuries are often dismissed as minor — but they can be catastrophic. A fall on a wet grocery store floor, a crumbling sidewalk, an unlit stairwell, or an icy parking lot can cause traumatic brain injuries, hip fractures, spinal cord damage, and shattered wrists and ankles that require surgery and months of rehabilitation. For older adults, a serious fall can be life-threatening. These are not accidents — they are the predictable result of property owners who fail to maintain safe conditions for the people on their premises.

Premises liability law requires property owners and occupiers to exercise reasonable care in maintaining their property and warning visitors of known hazards. But the specific legal standards vary significantly across Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia. Louisiana applies a merchant liability statute that requires the injured person to prove the condition existed for a sufficient time that the owner should have discovered and corrected it. Alabama uses a traditional premises liability framework with different duties owed to invitees, licensees, and trespassers. Georgia requires proof that the owner had actual or constructive knowledge of the hazard.

Insurance companies aggressively defend slip and fall claims. They will argue that you were not paying attention, that the hazard was open and obvious, that you were wearing inappropriate footwear, or that the property owner had no notice of the condition. Defeating these defenses requires thorough investigation: securing surveillance footage before it is overwritten, photographing the scene, identifying witnesses, reviewing maintenance logs, and retaining experts who can establish that the property owner's negligence caused the dangerous condition.

Cases We Handle

Types of Premises Liability Cases

We represent victims of serious injuries caused by negligent property maintenance, inadequate security, and hazardous conditions.

Wet Floor Injuries

Spills, leaks, freshly mopped surfaces, and rainwater tracked into stores and restaurants without warning signs or barriers. Grocery stores, big-box retailers, and restaurants are frequent locations for these preventable injuries.

Broken Stairs & Walkways

Cracked sidewalks, broken handrails, uneven flooring, deteriorated steps, and poorly maintained walkways in commercial buildings, apartment complexes, and public spaces that cause trips and falls.

Inadequate Lighting

Parking lots, stairwells, hallways, and entryways with burned-out lights, insufficient illumination, or obstructed visibility that prevent visitors from seeing and avoiding hazards.

Negligent Security

Assaults and criminal attacks on properties with inadequate security measures — broken locks, missing cameras, understaffed security, or poorly lit areas that the property owner knew were dangerous.

Common Questions

Slip and Fall FAQ

How do I prove the property owner was negligent?

You must generally show that the property owner knew or should have known about the dangerous condition and failed to fix it or warn you about it. Evidence can include surveillance footage showing how long the hazard existed, maintenance logs showing deferred repairs, witness testimony, incident reports filed with management, and photographs of the condition. Our attorneys move quickly to preserve this evidence before it is lost or destroyed.

What if the property owner says the hazard was obvious?

The "open and obvious" defense is one of the most common arguments property owners raise. However, this defense does not automatically bar your claim in many situations. A hazard may be open and obvious in theory but practically unavoidable — such as the only entrance to a store. The law also considers whether the property owner should have anticipated that visitors might be distracted or unable to avoid the hazard. Our attorneys know how to counter this defense effectively.

Should I report the fall to the property owner or manager?

Yes. Report the incident immediately and ask that an incident report be created. Request a copy of the report if possible. Also document the scene yourself — take photographs of the hazard, your injuries, and the surrounding area. Get the names and contact information of any witnesses. Seek medical attention promptly, even if your injuries seem minor at first, as some injuries worsen significantly over the following days.

How long do I have to file a slip and fall claim?

Louisiana allows one year from the date of the injury to file a premises liability lawsuit. Alabama and Georgia each allow two years. These deadlines are strictly enforced. Additionally, evidence in slip and fall cases — especially surveillance footage — is often overwritten within days or weeks. Contacting an attorney quickly is critical to preserving the evidence needed to prove your case.
