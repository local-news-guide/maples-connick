Fighting for Brain Injury Victims and Their Families

Traumatic brain injuries are among the most devastating and misunderstood injuries in personal injury law. A TBI can result from a car crash, a truck accident, a fall, a workplace explosion, a sports impact, or any event that causes the brain to strike the inside of the skull or to be penetrated by an object. The consequences can range from persistent headaches, memory loss, and cognitive difficulties to permanent disability, personality changes, seizures, and the inability to live independently. Unlike a broken bone that heals on a predictable timeline, brain injuries often worsen over time, and their full impact may not become apparent for months or years after the initial trauma.

Insurance companies routinely undervalue brain injury claims. They point to "normal" CT scans and MRIs to argue that the injury is not as severe as the victim claims, ignoring the well-documented reality that many brain injuries are not visible on standard imaging. They minimize cognitive and behavioral symptoms as subjective complaints. They offer quick settlements calculated on short-term medical costs, deliberately excluding the decades of treatment, rehabilitation, assisted living, and lost earning capacity that a TBI victim will actually need.

Cases We Handle

Types of Brain Injury Cases

We represent victims of traumatic brain injuries caused by negligence, from mild concussions with lasting effects to severe TBIs requiring lifetime care.

Severe Traumatic Brain Injury

Penetrating injuries, skull fractures, and closed-head trauma causing permanent cognitive impairment, personality changes, seizure disorders, or coma. These cases involve the highest lifetime care costs and the most significant losses.

Concussion & Mild TBI

So-called "mild" TBIs and concussions that cause persistent post-concussion syndrome — ongoing headaches, memory problems, difficulty concentrating, mood changes, and light sensitivity that can last months or years and profoundly affect daily life.

Diffuse Axonal Injury

Acceleration-deceleration injuries that shear nerve fibers throughout the brain, commonly caused by high-speed car crashes and truck accidents. These injuries may not appear on standard CT scans but can cause devastating, permanent neurological damage.

Anoxic & Hypoxic Brain Injury

Brain damage caused by oxygen deprivation — from near-drowning, medical negligence during surgery, birth injuries, or carbon monoxide exposure. Even minutes without adequate oxygen can cause permanent cognitive and physical disability.

Common Questions

Brain Injury FAQ

How do I know if I have a traumatic brain injury?

Symptoms of a TBI may include headaches, dizziness, confusion, memory problems, difficulty concentrating, mood swings, irritability, sleep disturbances, sensitivity to light or noise, nausea, and changes in personality or behavior. Some symptoms appear immediately after the injury, while others develop gradually over days or weeks. If you have experienced a blow to the head or a violent jolt — even if you did not lose consciousness — seek medical evaluation from a neurologist or other specialist. Early diagnosis is critical for both your health and your legal claim.

Can I have a brain injury even if my CT scan or MRI is normal?

Yes. Standard CT scans and conventional MRIs are designed to detect bleeding, fractures, and large structural abnormalities. They frequently miss the microscopic nerve damage that causes many traumatic brain injuries, particularly diffuse axonal injuries and mild TBIs. Advanced neuroimaging techniques — including diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), functional MRI, and SPECT scans — can reveal brain damage that standard imaging misses. Neuropsychological testing can also document cognitive deficits that confirm a brain injury.

What types of compensation are available for brain injury victims?

Brain injury victims may recover compensation for past and future medical expenses (including neurological treatment, rehabilitation, therapy, and medications), lost wages and diminished earning capacity, home modifications and assisted living costs, pain and suffering, loss of enjoyment of life, and emotional distress. In severe cases, a life-care plan prepared by a qualified expert will project the total lifetime cost of care, which can run into millions of dollars.

Why are brain injury cases so difficult to win?

Brain injuries are invisible. Unlike a broken bone visible on an X-ray or a wound that can be photographed, the damage from a TBI often cannot be seen on standard medical imaging. Insurance companies exploit this by arguing that the injury does not exist or is not as severe as claimed. Winning a brain injury case requires specialized medical evidence, expert testimony from neurologists and neuropsychologists, advanced imaging studies, and often compelling day-in-the-life videos that show the jury exactly how the injury has affected the victim's daily existence.
