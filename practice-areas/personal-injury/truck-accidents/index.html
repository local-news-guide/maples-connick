Holding Trucking Companies Accountable

A fully loaded 18-wheeler can weigh 80,000 pounds — more than 20 times the weight of a typical passenger car. When a commercial truck collides with a car, SUV, or pickup, the results are predictably catastrophic: crushed vehicles, traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, amputations, severe burns, and death. Truck accident cases are fundamentally different from car accident cases because of the severity of the injuries, the complexity of the regulations, and the number of potentially liable parties.

The federal government, through the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA), imposes detailed regulations on commercial trucking operations. These rules govern hours of service, driver qualifications, vehicle maintenance and inspection, cargo securement, drug and alcohol testing, and electronic logging device requirements. When a trucking company or driver violates these regulations — by pushing drivers past their legal hours, deferring required maintenance, or hiring unqualified operators — and that violation causes a crash, the company can be held liable for the resulting injuries.

Truck accident cases almost always involve multiple defendants: the truck driver, the trucking company, the vehicle owner, the cargo loader, the maintenance provider, and sometimes the truck or parts manufacturer. Each defendant has its own insurance carrier and defense team. The trucking company will often dispatch its own investigators and attorneys to the crash scene within hours, working to preserve evidence favorable to them while the injured victim is still in the hospital. Time is critical in these cases — evidence must be preserved before it is destroyed or lost.

Cases We Handle

Types of Truck Accident Cases

We handle the most serious commercial vehicle crashes involving regulatory violations, corporate negligence, and catastrophic injuries.

18-Wheeler Collisions

Tractor-trailer crashes on interstates and highways caused by driver fatigue, distraction, speeding, or impairment. These crashes often result in the most catastrophic injuries due to the massive size and weight disparity.

Hours-of-Service Violations

Trucking companies that push drivers past their federally mandated rest periods to meet delivery deadlines. Fatigued driving is one of the leading causes of commercial truck crashes in the Gulf South and Southeast.

Cargo & Overload Accidents

Improperly loaded, unsecured, or overweight cargo that shifts during transport, causing rollovers, jackknifes, and spilled loads. Cargo loaders and brokers may share liability with the trucking company.

Maintenance Failures

Brake failures, tire blowouts, steering defects, and other mechanical breakdowns caused by deferred maintenance or defective parts. Federal regulations require regular inspections — when companies skip them, people die.

Common Questions

Truck Accident FAQ

Why are truck accident cases more complex than car accident cases?

Truck accident cases involve federal FMCSA regulations, multiple potentially liable parties (driver, trucking company, cargo loader, maintenance provider, manufacturer), corporate insurance policies with higher limits, and critical electronic evidence like black box data and ELD logs. The trucking company will deploy its own investigators immediately after a crash, which means the injured party needs experienced legal representation right away to preserve evidence and protect their rights.

Who can be held liable in a truck accident?

Potentially liable parties include the truck driver, the trucking company that employed or contracted the driver, the owner of the truck or trailer, the company that loaded the cargo, the maintenance provider, and in some cases the manufacturer of the truck or its components. Each party may have separate insurance coverage, which can significantly increase the total compensation available to the injured person.

What evidence is critical in a truck accident case?

Electronic logging device (ELD) data, the truck's event data recorder (black box), driver qualification files, hours-of-service logs, maintenance and inspection records, dispatch communications, drug and alcohol test results, and cargo loading documentation. Much of this evidence is controlled by the trucking company and can be destroyed if a spoliation letter is not sent promptly. Acting quickly after a crash is essential.

How soon should I contact an attorney after a truck accident?

Immediately. Trucking companies routinely send their own investigators and defense teams to the crash scene within hours. Critical electronic evidence can be overwritten or destroyed if it is not preserved through a legal hold. The sooner you contact Maples & Connick, the sooner we can send a spoliation letter, begin our independent investigation, and protect your right to full compensation.
