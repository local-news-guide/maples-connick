Our Practice

Holding Negligent Drivers Accountable

Auto accidents are the leading cause of catastrophic personal injury across the Gulf South and Southeast. Rear-end collisions on I-10, intersection crashes in downtown New Orleans, high-speed highway pileups on I-65 in Alabama, and distracted driving wrecks on Georgia's interstates — these are the cases Maples & Connick handles every day. When a negligent driver causes a serious crash, the consequences extend far beyond the collision itself: traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, crushed limbs, and in the worst cases, death.

Insurance companies respond to serious auto accident claims with a well-rehearsed playbook. They dispute liability, downplay your injuries, pressure you into quick settlements, and deploy adjusters whose sole job is to minimize what they pay. Our attorneys know this playbook because we have fought against it for years. We conduct independent accident investigations, retain biomechanical and medical experts, and build cases designed to withstand the tactics that insurers use to reduce or deny legitimate claims.

Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia each apply different legal standards to motor vehicle accident cases. Louisiana uses a pure comparative fault system under its civil code. Alabama applies contributory negligence — one of the strictest standards in the country — which can bar recovery entirely if the injured party bears any fault. Georgia follows a modified comparative fault rule. These differences have a direct impact on case strategy, and choosing an attorney who understands all three systems can be the difference between full compensation and no recovery at all.

Cases We Handle

Types of Auto Accident Cases

We represent victims of serious motor vehicle crashes involving negligence, recklessness, and insurance bad faith.

Rear-End Collisions

Distracted, speeding, or tailgating drivers who slam into the back of your vehicle. These crashes frequently cause whiplash, herniated discs, and traumatic brain injuries.

Intersection Crashes

T-bone collisions, red-light runners, and failure-to-yield accidents at intersections. Side-impact crashes are among the most dangerous because vehicle doors provide less protection than front or rear crumple zones.

Highway Pileups

Multi-vehicle crashes on interstates and highways involving high speeds, chain-reaction impacts, and multiple at-fault parties. These cases require complex liability analysis and often involve commercial vehicles.

Uninsured Motorist Claims

When the at-fault driver has no insurance or insufficient coverage, your own UM/UIM policy may be your only source of compensation. We fight your insurer when they refuse to pay what the policy requires.

Common Questions

Auto Accident FAQ

What should I do immediately after a car accident?

Call 911 and seek medical attention, even if you feel fine — some injuries take hours or days to manifest. Document the scene with photographs, exchange information with the other driver, and get contact details from witnesses. Do not admit fault or apologize at the scene. Do not give a recorded statement to any insurance company before consulting an attorney.

How long do I have to file a car accident lawsuit?

The statute of limitations varies by state. Louisiana generally allows one year from the date of the accident. Alabama has a two-year statute of limitations, and Georgia also allows two years for most personal injury claims. Missing these deadlines can permanently bar your right to compensation, so contact an attorney as soon as possible after the crash.

What if the other driver does not have insurance?

If the at-fault driver is uninsured or underinsured, you may still recover compensation through your own uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage. Louisiana law requires insurers to offer UM coverage. Our attorneys handle UM claims against your own insurance company when they refuse to pay the full value of your policy.

Will the insurance company offer me a fair settlement?

Almost never on the first offer. Insurance adjusters are trained to minimize payouts. Their initial offer rarely accounts for future medical treatment, long-term lost earning capacity, or the full extent of pain and suffering. Having a trial attorney on your side signals to the insurer that you are prepared to go to court if they do not offer fair compensation.
