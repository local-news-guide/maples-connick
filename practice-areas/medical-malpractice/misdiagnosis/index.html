Diagnostic Errors

When Doctors Get It Wrong

A misdiagnosis or delayed diagnosis can be as devastating as any surgical error. When a physician fails to correctly identify a condition — or identifies it too late — the disease progresses, treatment options narrow, and outcomes worsen. Cancer that could have been treated in Stage I becomes terminal by Stage IV. A stroke misidentified as a migraine causes permanent brain damage that early intervention could have prevented.

Diagnostic error is the most common type of medical malpractice claim in the United States. At Maples & Connick, we handle cases involving missed cancer diagnoses, delayed stroke treatment, undetected heart conditions, misinterpreted lab results, and failure to order appropriate diagnostic tests. Each case requires detailed analysis of the differential diagnosis process — what the physician considered, what they should have considered, and what a competent physician in the same specialty would have done.

We work with board-certified physicians in the relevant specialty to establish the standard of care and demonstrate how the diagnostic failure caused additional harm. In Louisiana, these claims go through the medical review panel process before reaching court. In Alabama and Georgia, expert affidavits must accompany the initial filing.

Time limits are critical in diagnostic error cases. Louisiana allows one year from discovery of the misdiagnosis; Alabama and Georgia allow two years. If you believe a diagnostic error harmed you or a family member, call 504-269-3870 for a free consultation. We handle all cases on contingency.

Cases We Handle

Types of Diagnostic Error Cases

Cancer Misdiagnosis

Failure to diagnose breast cancer, lung cancer, colorectal cancer, melanoma, and other malignancies when symptoms, imaging, or lab results warranted further investigation.

Missed Stroke or Heart Attack

Emergency room failures to recognize stroke symptoms (FAST criteria) or cardiac events, leading to delayed treatment and permanent damage.

Failure to Order Tests

Physicians who skip appropriate diagnostic tests — biopsies, MRIs, blood panels, EKGs — when clinical presentation warranted them.

Misread Lab or Imaging Results

Radiologists, pathologists, or ordering physicians who misinterpret imaging studies, biopsy samples, or laboratory results.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a misdiagnosis and a delayed diagnosis?

A misdiagnosis means the physician identified the wrong condition (diagnosing pneumonia when the patient had lung cancer). A delayed diagnosis means the correct condition was eventually identified, but not soon enough — the delay allowed the disease to progress and caused additional harm that earlier treatment could have prevented.

How do you prove a misdiagnosis caused harm?

We must show that a competent physician in the same specialty, given the same information, would have reached the correct diagnosis sooner. Then we must prove that earlier diagnosis would have changed the outcome — better survival odds, less invasive treatment, or prevention of disability.

Can I sue if my cancer was caught late but I survived?

Yes. Even if you survived, a delayed cancer diagnosis can result in more aggressive treatment (additional chemotherapy, radiation, surgery), permanent side effects, higher medical costs, and reduced life expectancy. These are compensable harms under malpractice law.
