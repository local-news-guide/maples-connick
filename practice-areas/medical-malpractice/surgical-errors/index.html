Surgical Malpractice

Holding Surgeons Accountable

Surgical errors are among the most devastating forms of medical malpractice. When a surgeon operates on the wrong body part, leaves instruments or sponges inside a patient, administers improper anesthesia, or fails to manage post-operative complications, the consequences can be catastrophic — permanent disability, additional corrective surgeries, organ damage, infection, and death.

These cases are not simple. Proving surgical malpractice requires demonstrating that the surgeon or surgical team deviated from the accepted standard of care and that this deviation directly caused your injury. At Maples & Connick, we retain board-certified surgical experts who review operative reports, anesthesia records, nursing notes, and pathology findings to identify exactly where the standard of care was breached.

In Louisiana, medical malpractice claims must first be submitted to a medical review panel before a lawsuit can be filed in court. This panel consists of three physicians who review the evidence and issue an opinion on whether malpractice occurred. The panel process adds time and complexity, but it does not prevent you from pursuing your case — and a favorable panel opinion significantly strengthens your position. Alabama and Georgia do not require review panels but have their own procedural requirements, including expert affidavit requirements at filing.

Louisiana’s prescriptive period for medical malpractice is one year from the date of discovery of the malpractice (with a three-year outer limit). Alabama and Georgia both allow two years. Cases We Handle

Types of Surgical Error Cases

Wrong-Site Surgery

Operating on the wrong body part, wrong side, or wrong patient. These are classified as "never events" — errors that should never occur under any circumstance.

Retained Surgical Instruments

Sponges, needles, clamps, and other instruments left inside patients after surgery. These foreign bodies cause infection, pain, organ perforation, and often require additional surgery to remove.

Anesthesia Errors

Wrong dosage, failure to monitor vital signs, intubation injuries, and anesthesia awareness (patient waking during surgery). Anesthesia errors can cause brain damage, cardiac arrest, and death.

Post-Operative Negligence

Failure to monitor patients after surgery, delayed response to complications, premature discharge, and inadequate wound care. Post-op negligence can turn a successful surgery into a fatal outcome.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if a surgical error caused my injury?

If your condition worsened after surgery, you experienced unexpected complications, or you learned that an error occurred during the procedure, you may have a surgical malpractice claim. An attorney can obtain your medical records and have them reviewed by an independent surgical expert to determine whether the standard of care was met.

What is a medical review panel in Louisiana?

Louisiana law requires most medical malpractice claims to be submitted to a three-physician review panel before filing a lawsuit. The panel reviews evidence and issues a non-binding opinion on whether malpractice occurred. The process typically takes 12-18 months. While the opinion is advisory, it carries significant weight in subsequent litigation.

What damages can I recover in a surgical error case?

You may recover compensation for additional medical treatment, corrective surgeries, lost wages, future medical costs, pain and suffering, and loss of quality of life. In cases involving death, the family may pursue a wrongful death claim. Louisiana caps general damages in medical malpractice cases at $500,000, though this cap has been subject to legal challenges.
