Five Quick Tips to Prepare for Hurricane Season

Preparedness

May 4, 2024

By Aaron N. Maples

Hurricane season runs from June 1 through November 30 every year, and for those of us living along the Gulf Coast, preparation is not optional. New Orleans, Mobile, and the broader coastal regions of Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia face significant risk of wind damage, flooding, and storm surge with every hurricane season.

The best time to prepare is before the season starts. Here are five practical steps you can take now to protect yourself, your family, and your property — along with guidance on what to do if storm damage occurs.

Tip 1: Review Your Insurance Coverage

Before a storm hits, review your homeowner's insurance policy carefully. Many homeowners are surprised to learn what their standard policy does and does not cover.

What Standard Homeowner's Insurance Typically Covers

  • Wind damage to the structure and contents
  • Damage from falling trees and debris
  • Additional living expenses if you are displaced

What Standard Homeowner's Insurance Typically Does NOT Cover

  • Flood damage. This is the most important gap. Standard homeowner's policies do not cover flooding. Flood insurance is a separate policy, typically through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or a private insurer. If you do not have flood insurance and your home floods during a hurricane, you will not be covered.
  • Storm surge. Storm surge is technically a flood event, not a wind event. Without flood insurance, storm surge damage is not covered.

Key Things to Check

  • Your wind/hurricane deductible, which is often a percentage of the home's insured value (e.g., 2% or 5%), not a flat dollar amount
  • Whether your policy covers replacement cost or actual cash value (replacement cost is significantly better)
  • Your coverage limits — are they sufficient to rebuild your home at current construction costs?
  • Whether you have flood insurance, and if so, the coverage limits and deductible

If you need to purchase or increase flood insurance, do it now. NFIP policies have a 30-day waiting period before coverage takes effect. Once a storm is in the forecast, it is too late.

Tip 2: Document Your Property

Before a storm, take the time to thoroughly document your property and belongings. This documentation will be invaluable if you need to file an insurance claim.

  • Photograph or video every room in your home, including closets, garages, and storage areas
  • Document valuable items individually, including electronics, appliances, furniture, jewelry, and artwork
  • Keep receipts and appraisals for major purchases and high-value items
  • Create a written inventory listing items, approximate values, and purchase dates
  • Photograph the exterior of your home, including the roof, siding, windows, and landscaping

Store copies of this documentation in a cloud-based service or with a trusted person who lives outside the storm zone. Physical copies stored in your home are useless if your home is destroyed.

Tip 3: Prepare Your Home

Physical preparation of your home can reduce damage and protect your family. Focus on these areas:

Windows and Doors

Install hurricane shutters or have plywood pre-cut and labeled for each window. Reinforce garage doors, which are a common failure point during hurricanes. Consider impact-resistant windows if you are in a high-risk zone.

Roof

Have your roof inspected before hurricane season. Replace missing or damaged shingles. Secure loose flashing and trim trees that overhang the roof.

Yard

Secure or store outdoor furniture, grills, potted plants, and any objects that could become projectiles in high winds. Trim dead branches from trees near your home. Clean gutters and downspouts so water drains properly.

Generators and Supplies

If you have a generator, test it before the season starts and stock fuel. Never operate a generator indoors — carbon monoxide poisoning is a leading cause of death after hurricanes. Stock basic supplies: water (one gallon per person per day for at least three days), non-perishable food, medications, flashlights, batteries, and a battery-powered radio.

Tip 4: Make an Evacuation Plan

Know your evacuation zone and your evacuation route. Do not wait for a mandatory evacuation order to start planning.

  • Identify where you will go. Have a primary destination and a backup. If you are going to a hotel, make reservations early — rooms fill up quickly when a storm is approaching.
  • Plan for pets. Not all shelters accept animals. Identify pet-friendly hotels or shelters along your evacuation route.
  • Prepare a go-bag with essential documents (insurance policies, identification, medical records), medications, phone chargers, cash, and clothing for several days.
  • Fill your gas tank before a storm is imminent. Gas stations run out of fuel quickly during evacuations.
  • Communicate your plan with family members who do not live with you, so everyone knows where you are going.

Tip 5: Know What to Do After the Storm

The decisions you make in the days after a hurricane can affect your ability to recover insurance benefits and pursue legal claims. Here is what to do if your property is damaged:

Document the Damage Immediately

Before cleaning up or making repairs, photograph and video all damage. Capture structural damage, water lines, damaged contents, and any debris. This documentation is critical for your insurance claim.

Prevent Further Damage

Your insurance policy requires you to take reasonable steps to prevent additional damage. This includes tarping a damaged roof, boarding broken windows, and removing standing water. Keep receipts for any emergency repairs or supplies — these costs are typically reimbursable under your policy.

File Your Claim Promptly

Contact your insurance company as soon as possible to report the damage. Keep a written record of every communication, including the name of the person you spoke with, the date, and what was discussed.

Do Not Accept the First Offer Without Review

Insurance companies process thousands of claims after a hurricane, and initial assessments are often low. You have the right to dispute the insurer's estimate. Consider getting your own independent estimate from a licensed contractor.

Be Cautious with Contractors

After a hurricane, unlicensed and unscrupulous contractors flood affected areas. Get multiple estimates, verify licenses and insurance, check references, and never pay the full amount upfront. Be especially wary of contractors who ask you to sign over your insurance benefits — this practice, known as assignment of benefits, can limit your control over your claim.

When to Contact an Attorney

Most hurricane-related insurance claims are resolved without legal intervention. However, there are situations where an attorney can help:

  • Your claim is denied and you believe the denial is unjustified
  • The insurance company's estimate is significantly lower than the actual cost of repairs
  • The insurer is delaying processing your claim or not responding to communications
  • You have a dispute about whether damage was caused by wind (covered) or flood (separate policy)
  • Your property was damaged due to someone else's negligence, such as a tree from a neighbor's property that they were warned about

Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia each have specific laws governing insurance claims and bad faith insurance practices. If your insurer is not treating your claim fairly, you may have legal recourse beyond the claim itself.

Prepare Now

Hurricane preparation is about reducing risk and positioning yourself to recover as quickly as possible if a storm does hit. The steps above are straightforward and can be completed in a single weekend. The peace of mind they provide is worth the effort.
