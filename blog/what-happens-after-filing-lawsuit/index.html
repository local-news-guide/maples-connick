The Road Ahead: What Happens After Filing a Lawsuit

Civil Litigation

May 20, 2024

By Aaron N. Maples

You have decided to file a lawsuit. Maybe you were injured in a car accident, harmed by medical negligence, or hurt by someone else's carelessness. Your attorney has investigated the facts, evaluated your claim, and determined that litigation is the right path. The petition or complaint has been drafted and filed with the court.

Now what?

For most people, filing a lawsuit is unfamiliar territory. The legal process can seem opaque and intimidating. Understanding what happens after the complaint is filed can reduce anxiety, set realistic expectations, and help you make better decisions along the way.

Stage 1: Service of Process

After the complaint is filed, it must be served on the defendant — meaning the defendant must be formally notified of the lawsuit and given a copy of the complaint. This is called service of process, and it is a constitutional requirement. A lawsuit does not truly begin until the defendant has been properly served.

Service is typically accomplished by a sheriff, process server, or certified mail, depending on the jurisdiction and the type of defendant. In Louisiana, service requirements are governed by the Code of Civil Procedure. In Alabama and Georgia, each state has its own rules.

Once served, the defendant has a set period of time to respond — typically 15 to 30 days, depending on the state and the type of case. The response may be an answer (admitting or denying the allegations) or a motion (such as a motion to dismiss the case).

Stage 2: Pleadings and Early Motions

The initial phase of litigation involves the exchange of formal documents called pleadings. The plaintiff files the complaint. The defendant files an answer. The defendant may also file a counterclaim against the plaintiff, a crossclaim against a co-defendant, or a third-party demand bringing a new party into the case.

This phase may also involve early motions. Common ones include:

  • Motion to dismiss: The defendant argues that the complaint fails to state a valid legal claim, even if all the facts alleged are true.
  • Motion for more definite statement: The defendant argues that the complaint is too vague to respond to.
  • Motion to transfer venue: The defendant argues that the case was filed in the wrong court or that another court is more appropriate.

These motions are resolved by the judge, usually after written briefs and sometimes after oral argument. If the case survives the early motion stage, it moves into discovery.

Stage 3: Discovery

Discovery is the longest and most labor-intensive phase of litigation. It is the process by which each side gathers evidence from the other side and from third parties. Discovery typically lasts months and in complex cases can take a year or more.

Interrogatories

Written questions sent from one party to another, requiring written answers under oath. Interrogatories are used to gather factual information about the other side's claims, defenses, witnesses, and damages.

Requests for Production of Documents

Formal requests for relevant documents, including medical records, insurance policies, employment records, communications, photographs, and any other documents relevant to the case.

Depositions

Depositions are live, in-person (or virtual) question-and-answer sessions conducted under oath. The testimony is recorded by a court reporter and can be used at trial. Depositions are often the most revealing part of discovery, because they allow attorneys to question witnesses in real time and probe for inconsistencies.

In a personal injury case, the plaintiff, the defendant, medical providers, expert witnesses, and eyewitnesses may all be deposed. Your deposition as the plaintiff is one of the most important events in the case. Your attorney will prepare you thoroughly for what to expect.

Requests for Admission

Written statements sent from one party to the other, asking the recipient to admit or deny specific facts. Admissions narrow the issues for trial and can streamline the case.

Independent Medical Examinations

In personal injury cases, the defendant typically has the right to have you examined by a doctor of their choosing. This is called an independent medical examination (IME), though the term is somewhat misleading — the doctor is selected and paid by the defense. The purpose is to assess the nature and extent of your injuries from the defendant's perspective.

Stage 4: Expert Witnesses

In most personal injury and medical malpractice cases, both sides retain expert witnesses. Experts are professionals — doctors, economists, accident reconstructionists, life care planners — who provide opinions on technical issues that are beyond the knowledge of ordinary jurors.

Common expert witnesses in injury cases include:

  • Medical experts who testify about the nature, cause, and prognosis of your injuries
  • Economic experts who calculate lost earnings and future earning capacity
  • Life care planners who estimate the cost of future medical treatment and care
  • Accident reconstructionists who analyze how the accident occurred
  • Vocational experts who assess your ability to return to work

Expert reports are exchanged during discovery, and experts are typically deposed by the opposing side. The quality and credibility of your expert witnesses can significantly affect the outcome of your case.

Stage 5: Motions for Summary Judgment

After discovery is complete, either side may file a motion for summary judgment. This motion asks the judge to decide the case (or specific issues within the case) without a trial, on the grounds that there is no genuine dispute of material fact.

Summary judgment motions are common in personal injury litigation. The defendant may argue that the evidence clearly shows no negligence, or the plaintiff may argue that liability is undisputed and only damages need to be determined at trial. The judge evaluates the evidence in the light most favorable to the non-moving party.

Stage 6: Mediation and Settlement Negotiations

Most courts require the parties to participate in mediation or some form of alternative dispute resolution before trial. Mediation is a facilitated negotiation session conducted by a neutral third party (the mediator). The mediator does not decide the case but helps the parties explore settlement.

Many cases settle at or shortly after mediation. The settlement discussion at this stage is informed by the evidence gathered during discovery, the expert reports, and both sides' realistic assessment of what might happen at trial.

Settlement is always the plaintiff's decision. Your attorney will advise you, but the choice of whether to accept a settlement offer or proceed to trial is ultimately yours.

Stage 7: Pre-Trial Preparation

If the case does not settle, it proceeds to trial. Pre-trial preparation includes:

  • Filing pre-trial motions (motions in limine) to exclude or include specific evidence
  • Preparing jury instructions
  • Organizing exhibits and demonstrative aids
  • Finalizing witness lists and preparing witnesses for testimony
  • Conducting mock jury exercises or focus groups

This phase is intensive. A well-prepared trial presentation requires careful organization of the evidence, a clear case narrative, and thorough preparation of every witness.

Stage 8: Trial

At trial, both sides present their evidence to a judge or jury. The plaintiff presents first, calling witnesses and introducing exhibits. The defendant then presents their case. Both sides have the opportunity to cross-examine the other side's witnesses.

After both sides have presented their evidence, they deliver closing arguments. In a jury trial, the judge then instructs the jury on the applicable law, and the jury deliberates and returns a verdict.

Trials in personal injury cases typically last three to ten days, depending on the complexity of the case. Medical malpractice trials tend to be longer due to the volume of medical evidence and expert testimony.

Stage 9: Post-Trial

After a verdict, either side may file post-trial motions, including motions for a new trial or motions to alter the judgment. If post-trial motions are denied, the losing party may appeal to a higher court. Appeals can add months or years to the timeline.

If you win a verdict and the defendant does not appeal (or the appeal is resolved in your favor), the judgment is enforceable. Collection of the judgment may involve additional legal steps if the defendant does not pay voluntarily.

How Long Does All of This Take?

The timeline varies significantly depending on the complexity of the case, the court's docket, and the parties' litigation strategies. A straightforward personal injury case may resolve in 12 to 18 months. A complex medical malpractice case (especially in Louisiana, where a medical review panel is required) may take two to four years or longer.

Throughout this process, your attorney should keep you informed about the status of your case, explain what is happening at each stage, and involve you in key decisions.
