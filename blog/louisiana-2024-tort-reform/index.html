Louisiana's 2024 Reform: Timing is Everything

Tort Reform

August 21, 2025

Maples & Connick

In 2024, the Louisiana Legislature passed a series of tort reform measures that changed the landscape for personal injury litigation in the state. These changes affect everything from the prescriptive period for filing claims to the collateral source rule to jury trial thresholds. For anyone with a pending or potential personal injury claim in Louisiana, the timing of your case relative to these reforms is critical.

At Maples Connick, we have been tracking these legislative changes from the moment they were introduced. Here is a breakdown of the key provisions, what they mean for injured victims, and why timing matters more than ever.

Background: Why Louisiana Reformed Its Tort Laws

Louisiana has long been considered a plaintiff-friendly state for personal injury litigation. The state's civil law tradition, combined with comparatively generous damages awards and a low jury trial threshold, made Louisiana an outlier among its neighbors. Insurance industry groups and business associations had been lobbying for years to bring Louisiana more in line with other states.

The reform effort gained momentum in 2020 with the passage of several initial changes. The 2024 legislation represents the next wave, building on those earlier reforms and making additional modifications to the Civil Code and Code of Civil Procedure.

Key Provisions of the 2024 Reform

Prescriptive Period Changes

One of the most significant changes involves the prescriptive period (Louisiana's equivalent of a statute of limitations) for personal injury claims. Under the new provisions, the timeline for filing certain claims has been tightened. This means that injured victims have less time to bring their cases to court.

For anyone who has been injured, this change underscores the importance of consulting an attorney promptly. Delays in seeking legal advice can now more easily result in a time-barred claim. The one-year prescriptive period for delictual actions under La. C.C. art. 3492 remains in effect, but the calculation of when prescription begins to run has been refined in ways that can shorten the effective window.

Collateral Source Rule Modifications

The collateral source rule has traditionally prevented defendants from introducing evidence of payments the plaintiff received from other sources — such as health insurance, disability benefits, or workers' compensation. This rule ensured that a defendant could not reduce their liability simply because the plaintiff had the foresight to carry insurance.

The 2024 reforms modified this rule. Under the new framework, evidence of certain collateral source payments may now be admissible in some circumstances. The practical effect is that juries may learn about insurance payments and other benefits the plaintiff received, which can influence their assessment of damages.

This is a significant shift. In practice, it means that a plaintiff who had health insurance pay for treatment may see their damages award reduced because the jury is aware that the bills were already covered. The reform benefits defendants and insurers at the expense of injured plaintiffs.

Jury Trial Threshold

Louisiana previously had one of the lowest thresholds for jury trials in personal injury cases, allowing juries for claims as low as $10,000 (after the 2020 reform raised it from $50,000 to $10,000, then gradually back up). The 2024 reforms continued adjusting this threshold. The amount in controversy required for a jury trial was increased, meaning that more cases will now be decided by judges rather than juries.

Whether this benefits plaintiffs or defendants depends on the specific case and the specific judge. Historically, juries in Louisiana have been perceived as more generous than judges in personal injury cases, particularly in certain parishes. Raising the jury trial threshold effectively removes the jury option from smaller cases.

Direct Action Against Insurers

Louisiana's direct action statute has long allowed injured plaintiffs to sue an insurer directly, without first obtaining a judgment against the insured. This is a powerful tool because it allows the jury to know that an insurance company — not an individual — is the one paying the verdict.

The 2024 reforms imposed additional limitations on direct actions. While the statute still exists, the conditions under which a plaintiff can invoke it have been narrowed. This change reduces the strategic advantage that the direct action statute provided to plaintiffs.

Medical Expense Evidence

The reforms also addressed how medical expenses are presented at trial. Under previous law, plaintiffs could present the full amount of their medical bills as evidence of damages, regardless of what was actually paid. The new provisions adjust the rules around what constitutes admissible evidence of medical expenses, potentially limiting the amounts that juries see.

This change intersects with the collateral source modifications. Together, they create a framework where the actual cost of medical treatment — as reflected in insurance write-offs and negotiated rates — may play a larger role in determining damages.

Why Timing Matters

The applicability of these reforms depends on when the cause of action arose and when the lawsuit was filed. Most of the 2024 provisions apply prospectively, meaning they affect cases arising after the effective date of the legislation. However, the transition rules can be complex, and some provisions may affect pending litigation as well.

This creates a timing issue. If you were injured before the reforms took effect, your case may be governed by the prior, more plaintiff-friendly rules. If you were injured after the effective date, the new rules apply. If you were injured before but have not yet filed suit, the analysis becomes more nuanced.

The practical takeaway is clear: do not delay. If you have a potential personal injury claim in Louisiana, the sooner you consult an attorney, the better positioned you are to take advantage of the most favorable legal framework available.

Impact on Case Valuation

The combined effect of these reforms is to reduce the overall value of personal injury claims in Louisiana. Shortened prescriptive periods mean fewer claims are filed. Collateral source modifications reduce the damages juries award. Higher jury trial thresholds remove the jury option from smaller cases. Limitations on direct actions reduce strategic leverage against insurers.

None of these changes eliminate the right to bring a personal injury claim. But they collectively make it harder for injured victims to recover the full measure of their damages. Insurance companies and their attorneys are already citing the reforms in settlement negotiations to justify lower offers.

What This Means for You

If you have been injured in Louisiana, these reforms make it more important than ever to work with an attorney who understands the new landscape. At Maples Connick, we have adapted our litigation strategies to account for these changes. We know which provisions apply to which cases, how to navigate the transitional rules, and how to present cases effectively under the new framework.

The fundamentals of personal injury law have not changed: if someone's negligence caused your injury, you have a right to compensation. But the tools available to pursue that compensation have shifted, and having experienced counsel who understands those shifts is essential.
