Subrogation in Personal Injury Claims: What Injured Victims Need to Know

Insurance

August 22, 2025

Maples & Connick

You have been injured, you fought for a fair settlement, and now you finally have a check in hand. But before you can use that money to cover your bills and rebuild your life, your health insurance company sends a letter claiming a portion of your recovery. This is subrogation, and it catches many injury victims off guard.

Subrogation is a legal principle that allows an insurance company that has paid for your medical treatment to seek reimbursement from the settlement or verdict you receive from the party that caused your injuries. It is one of the most misunderstood aspects of personal injury law, and it can significantly reduce the amount of compensation you actually keep.

How Subrogation Works

When you are injured in an accident caused by someone else, your health insurance typically covers your initial medical treatment. Your auto insurance may also pay benefits. These insurers step in because you need care immediately, regardless of who was at fault.

Once you pursue a claim against the at-fault party and reach a settlement or win a verdict, those insurers have a legal right to recover what they paid on your behalf. The logic is straightforward: the at-fault party should bear the cost of your treatment, not your own insurer. Subrogation shifts that cost back to where it belongs.

In practice, this means your health insurer places a lien on your personal injury recovery. Before you receive your share of the settlement, the subrogation lien must be addressed. If it is not handled properly, it can consume a disproportionate share of your compensation.

Types of Subrogation Claims

Different types of insurance carriers exercise subrogation rights, and each operates under different legal frameworks:

Private Health Insurance

Most private health insurance policies contain subrogation clauses. These clauses give the insurer the right to recover payments it made for treatment related to your injury. The enforceability and scope of these clauses vary depending on whether the plan is governed by state law or federal law (ERISA).

ERISA Plans

If your health coverage comes through an employer-sponsored plan governed by the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA), the subrogation rules are typically more rigid. Federal courts have generally upheld the right of ERISA plans to full reimbursement, and many of the state-law protections that benefit injury victims do not apply to these plans.

Medicare and Medicaid

Both Medicare and Medicaid have strong subrogation rights backed by federal statute. Medicare's right to recovery is established under the Medicare Secondary Payer Act, and failing to properly address a Medicare lien can result in serious penalties. Medicaid programs operate under state-specific rules but similarly assert recovery rights.

Auto Insurance (Med Pay and PIP)

If your auto insurance paid medical payments (Med Pay) or personal injury protection (PIP) benefits, the insurer may have a subrogation claim depending on your state and your policy terms. Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia each handle auto insurance subrogation differently.

Subrogation in Louisiana

Louisiana law provides important protections for injury victims facing subrogation claims. Under Louisiana jurisprudence, the "made whole" doctrine requires that an injured person be fully compensated before a subrogation claim can be satisfied. This means that if your settlement does not make you whole, your health insurer may not be entitled to full reimbursement.

Additionally, Louisiana courts have recognized the "common fund" doctrine, which allows a reduction of the subrogation amount by a proportionate share of the attorney fees and costs incurred in obtaining the recovery. The rationale is that the insurer benefits from your attorney's work and should share in the expense.

These protections can meaningfully reduce the amount you owe your insurer and increase the amount you keep. However, as noted above, ERISA plans may be exempt from these state-law protections under federal preemption.

Subrogation in Alabama and Georgia

Alabama and Georgia also recognize subrogation rights but apply their own frameworks. In Alabama, the made whole doctrine has been recognized by courts, offering similar protections to those in Louisiana. Georgia courts have applied the doctrine as well, though the specifics can vary depending on the type of insurance and whether the plan is self-funded.

In all three states, the enforceability of a subrogation clause depends heavily on the language of the specific policy or plan. A careful review of your coverage documents is essential.

Why Subrogation Matters to Your Case

Subrogation is not a minor technical issue. In many personal injury cases, the subrogation lien represents tens or even hundreds of thousands of dollars. If you settle a case for $200,000 and your health insurer has a $75,000 subrogation claim, the impact on your net recovery is enormous.

Without proper negotiation, you could end up paying your insurer in full while keeping far less than you need to cover your ongoing medical expenses, lost income, and other damages. This is especially problematic in cases where the settlement does not fully compensate you for all of your losses.

How We Protect Your Recovery

At Maples Connick, we address subrogation from the beginning of every personal injury case, not as an afterthought. Our approach includes:

  • Early identification. We identify all potential subrogation claims early in the case so there are no surprises at settlement time.
  • Lien verification. We verify the accuracy of every lien amount. Insurers frequently include charges that are unrelated to the accident or that have been inflated.
  • Aggressive negotiation. We negotiate with subrogation carriers to reduce the amount you owe, using the made whole doctrine, common fund doctrine, and any other applicable legal tools.
  • ERISA analysis. For employer-sponsored plans, we analyze whether the plan is truly governed by ERISA and whether its subrogation provisions are enforceable under current case law.
  • Medicare compliance. For clients with Medicare, we ensure full compliance with the Medicare Secondary Payer Act while protecting as much of the recovery as possible.

Common Mistakes That Hurt Injury Victims

We frequently see cases where subrogation was handled poorly, often because it was not addressed until the last minute. Common mistakes include:

  • Settling a case without identifying all subrogation liens
  • Paying a subrogation lien in full without negotiating a reduction
  • Ignoring Medicare reporting requirements
  • Failing to assert the made whole doctrine when it applies
  • Not reviewing the plan language to determine whether ERISA preemption actually applies

Each of these mistakes reduces the amount you keep from your settlement. In some cases, the loss is substantial.

What You Should Do

If you have been injured and are pursuing a personal injury claim, make sure your attorney is addressing subrogation from the start. Ask about potential liens, inquire about the plan type (ERISA or state-regulated), and understand how subrogation will affect your net recovery.

Subrogation negotiation is a skill that requires knowledge of both insurance law and personal injury litigation. It is not enough to simply write a check to the insurer and move on. Proper handling of subrogation can mean the difference between a recovery that covers your needs and one that leaves you short.
