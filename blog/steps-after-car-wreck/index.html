Stay Calm: Steps to Take After a Car Wreck

Practical Guide

June 13, 2024

By Aaron N. Maples

A car wreck can happen in an instant, but the decisions you make in the hours and days that follow can affect your health, your finances, and your legal rights for years. Most people have never been in a serious accident before and do not know what to do. The adrenaline is pumping, you may be in pain, and the situation feels chaotic.

Here is a clear, step-by-step guide to what you should do after a car wreck. Following these steps can protect your health and preserve your right to recover compensation if someone else was at fault.

Step 1: Check for Injuries and Get to Safety

Your first priority is safety. Check yourself and your passengers for injuries. If anyone is seriously hurt, call 911 immediately. Do not attempt to move someone who may have a spinal injury unless there is an immediate danger, such as a fire.

If your vehicle is drivable and you can safely move it out of traffic, do so. Turn on your hazard lights. If you cannot move the vehicle, stay inside with your seatbelt on until help arrives, unless remaining in the vehicle is dangerous.

Step 2: Call the Police

Always call the police after a car accident, even if the accident seems minor. A police report creates an official record of the accident, including the officer's observations about the scene, the positions of the vehicles, statements from the drivers and witnesses, and any citations issued.

The police report is often one of the most important pieces of evidence in a personal injury claim. Insurance companies rely on it, and juries give it significant weight. Without a police report, you are left with a he-said-she-said situation that can be difficult to resolve.

In Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia, you are generally required to report accidents that result in injury, death, or property damage above a certain threshold. But as a practical matter, you should call the police after any accident that involves another vehicle.

Step 3: Exchange Information

Exchange the following information with the other driver:

  • Full name and contact information
  • Driver's license number
  • Insurance company name and policy number
  • Vehicle make, model, year, and color
  • License plate number

Be polite but do not discuss fault. Do not apologize for the accident. Anything you say can be used against you later. Stick to exchanging factual information.

Step 4: Document the Scene

If you are physically able, use your phone to document the accident scene. Take photographs of:

  • All vehicles involved, from multiple angles, showing the damage
  • The overall scene, including traffic signals, signs, lane markings, and road conditions
  • Skid marks, debris, or other physical evidence on the road
  • Your injuries (bruises, cuts, swelling)
  • The other driver's license plate and insurance card
  • Any visible damage to property (guardrails, fences, poles)

If there are witnesses, ask for their names and phone numbers. Witness testimony can be crucial in disputed liability cases.

Step 5: Seek Medical Attention

See a doctor as soon as possible after the accident, even if you feel fine. Many injuries — including whiplash, concussions, internal bleeding, and soft tissue injuries — do not produce immediate symptoms. Adrenaline can mask pain for hours or even days.

Prompt medical attention serves two purposes. First, it protects your health by identifying injuries that may not be immediately apparent. Second, it creates a medical record that links your injuries to the accident. If you wait days or weeks to see a doctor, the insurance company will argue that your injuries were not caused by the accident or that they are not as serious as you claim.

Follow your doctor's treatment plan. Attend all follow-up appointments. Keep records of every medical visit, prescription, and therapy session related to the accident.

Step 6: Notify Your Insurance Company

Report the accident to your own insurance company promptly. Most policies require timely notification of accidents. When you call, stick to the facts: when and where the accident happened, the other driver's information, and the police report number.

Do not give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurance company without first consulting an attorney. The other insurer's adjuster is trained to ask questions designed to minimize your claim. What seems like a routine conversation can produce statements that are used against you later.

Step 7: Keep a Record

Start a file for everything related to the accident. This should include:

  • The police report
  • All medical records and bills
  • Repair estimates and receipts
  • Correspondence with insurance companies
  • A journal documenting your pain levels, limitations, and emotional state in the days and weeks after the accident
  • Records of missed work and lost income

This documentation becomes the foundation of your claim. The more organized and thorough your records, the stronger your position when negotiating with the insurance company.

Step 8: Do Not Accept a Quick Settlement

Insurance companies often make early settlement offers, sometimes within days of the accident. These offers are almost always far below the actual value of the claim. The insurer is betting that you will accept a quick check before you understand the full extent of your injuries and damages.

Once you accept a settlement and sign a release, you cannot go back and ask for more money. If your injuries turn out to be worse than initially thought — which happens frequently — you are stuck with whatever you accepted. Do not sign anything or accept any payment without consulting an attorney.

Step 9: Consult a Personal Injury Attorney

If you have been injured in a car wreck caused by someone else's negligence, consult a personal injury attorney before making any decisions about your claim. Most personal injury attorneys offer free initial consultations and work on a contingency fee basis, meaning you pay nothing unless they recover compensation for you.

An experienced attorney can:

  • Evaluate the strength of your claim
  • Estimate the full value of your damages
  • Handle communications with the insurance company
  • Gather evidence and build your case
  • Negotiate a fair settlement or take your case to trial if necessary

Common Mistakes to Avoid

We see the same mistakes repeatedly in accident cases. Avoid these:

  • Waiting too long to see a doctor. Delays in treatment give the insurance company ammunition to dispute your injuries.
  • Posting about the accident on social media. Insurance adjusters routinely monitor plaintiffs' social media accounts. A post that seems harmless to you can be used to undermine your claim.
  • Giving a recorded statement to the other driver's insurer. You are not required to do this, and it rarely helps your case.
  • Accepting the first settlement offer. Early offers are designed to close the claim cheaply, not to compensate you fairly.
  • Not following through on medical treatment. Gaps in treatment suggest to the insurer that your injuries are not serious.
