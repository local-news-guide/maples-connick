Why Courtroom Experience Matters

Trial Practice

August 20, 2025

By Brendan P. Connick

Most personal injury cases settle before trial. That is a fact. But here is a fact that matters more: the cases that settle well do so because the insurance company knows the attorney on the other side is willing and able to try the case.

Insurance adjusters and defense attorneys evaluate every claim through a risk-assessment lens. One of the most important factors in that assessment is whether the plaintiff's attorney has real courtroom experience. When the answer is yes, the settlement calculus changes dramatically.

The Settlement Dynamic

Personal injury settlements are not charitable gestures from insurance companies. Every offer is a business calculation. The insurer weighs the probable cost of a trial verdict against the cost of settling now. If the insurer believes the case will never actually go to trial, there is little incentive to offer a fair settlement.

This dynamic creates a fundamental problem for attorneys who do not try cases. Without a credible trial threat, the attorney's negotiating leverage is limited. The insurance company knows it can offer a lowball settlement and the attorney will likely accept it because the alternative — actually going to court — is not in the attorney's skill set.

Trial attorneys operate from a different position entirely. When an insurance adjuster sees that the plaintiff's attorney has taken cases to verdict — and won — the risk assessment changes. The insurer must now account for the real possibility that this case will be tried, and that the verdict could substantially exceed the current settlement offer.

What Insurance Companies Track

Insurance companies maintain detailed profiles on plaintiff's attorneys. They track:

  • Trial history. How many cases has this attorney actually tried? What were the verdicts?
  • Case type experience. Has this attorney tried cases similar to the one at hand?
  • Jurisdiction knowledge. Does this attorney know the judges, the juries, and the procedural landscape in this venue?
  • Preparation quality. Are this attorney's cases well-organized, with strong expert witnesses and clear presentation?
  • Willingness to reject low offers. Has this attorney turned down settlements and gone to trial rather than accepting inadequate offers?

Attorneys who score high on these factors get better settlement offers. It is not a coincidence. It is a direct consequence of the risk they represent to the insurer.

The Courtroom Is Different

Trial work is a distinct skill set that cannot be learned from a textbook. The courtroom requires:

Jury Selection

Choosing the right jury is one of the most consequential decisions in a trial. Experienced trial attorneys know how to identify jurors who will be receptive to their client's case and how to use voir dire effectively. This is a skill built through repetition, not theory.

Opening and Closing Statements

The opening statement sets the narrative frame for the entire trial. The closing argument is the attorney's last opportunity to persuade the jury. Both require the ability to tell a compelling story under the constraints of evidence law. Effective trial attorneys have refined these skills over many cases.

Direct and Cross-Examination

Presenting your own witnesses and challenging the opposition's witnesses are fundamentally different skills. Direct examination requires the ability to draw out testimony in a clear, compelling sequence. Cross-examination requires the ability to control a hostile witness, expose weaknesses in their testimony, and do so without alienating the jury.

Evidence Presentation

Getting evidence admitted at trial requires knowledge of the rules of evidence, the ability to lay proper foundations, and the skill to present complex information — medical records, accident reconstruction, economic analyses — in a way that jurors can understand. This is the technical backbone of trial work.

Real-Time Adaptation

No trial goes exactly as planned. Witnesses say unexpected things. Judges make unexpected rulings. Opposing counsel raises unexpected arguments. The ability to adapt in real time, without losing the thread of the case, is what separates experienced trial attorneys from attorneys who merely know the rules.

The Preparation Advantage

Attorneys who try cases prepare differently than attorneys who plan to settle. Trial preparation is more thorough, more detailed, and more adversarial. It includes:

  • Deposing witnesses with trial testimony in mind, not just information gathering
  • Retaining expert witnesses who can withstand cross-examination, not just write favorable reports
  • Developing a case theme that works for a jury, not just a settlement memo
  • Preparing demonstrative exhibits, visual aids, and technology presentations
  • Conducting mock jury exercises or focus groups to test case theories

This level of preparation benefits the client even if the case settles. A thoroughly prepared case commands a higher settlement offer because the defense can see the strength of the presentation they will face at trial.

The Cost of Inexperience

When an attorney lacks courtroom experience, the consequences fall on the client. Inadequate settlement offers go unchallenged. Cases that should be tried are settled for less than they are worth. In the worst cases, a client ends up at trial with an attorney who is not equipped to handle it.

We have seen cases where clients came to us after being dissatisfied with their previous attorney's approach. In many of these situations, the prior attorney had never tried a case. The insurance company knew it, and the settlement offer reflected that knowledge.

Trial Experience Across Five States

Maples Connick litigates personal injury cases in Louisiana, Alabama, and Georgia. Each state has its own procedural rules, its own jury pool characteristics, and its own courtroom culture. What works in a New Orleans courtroom may not work in Mobile or Atlanta.

Our attorneys have tried cases in all five states. We understand the local rules, we know the judges, and we have experience with the juries in our jurisdictions. This multi-state trial experience is not common, and it gives us a distinct advantage when litigating cases across state lines.

Louisiana

Louisiana's civil law system creates unique procedural considerations. Bench trials are common for smaller cases due to the jury trial threshold. The Medical Malpractice Act requires a medical review panel before suit can be filed. Our attorneys navigate these procedures regularly.

Alabama

Alabama's contributory negligence standard makes trial presentation critical. Any finding of plaintiff fault can bar recovery entirely. This means every aspect of the trial — from jury selection to closing argument — must address the negligence question with precision.

Georgia

Georgia uses a modified comparative fault system and has its own pre-trial requirements for medical malpractice cases. Georgia juries can be receptive to strong plaintiff cases, but the presentation must be clear, professional, and well-supported by evidence.

Questions to Ask Your Attorney

If you are considering hiring a personal injury attorney, ask about their trial experience. Specific questions to consider:

  • How many cases have you tried to verdict?
  • When was your most recent trial?
  • Have you tried cases similar to mine?
  • Have you tried cases in this jurisdiction?
  • What percentage of your cases go to trial versus settling?

An attorney who is evasive about these questions may not have the courtroom experience your case requires. Trial experience is not something that can be faked, and it is not something that develops overnight.

The Bottom Line

Courtroom experience matters because it changes the entire trajectory of your case. It affects how the insurance company evaluates your claim, how much they offer in settlement, and what happens if the case goes to trial. An attorney with real trial experience is an attorney who can get you a fair result — whether that result comes from a negotiation table or a jury.

We prepare every case as if it will be tried, and we are ready to go to court when that is what it takes.
