The Case Three Firms Rejected: A $4.4 Million Industrial Accident Settlement

Case Study — Workplace Injury / Wrongful Death

Attorney: Aaron N. Maples

Three law firms looked at this case and walked away. The liability was murky, the defendants had deep pockets and aggressive defense teams, and the facts did not line up the way they needed to for an easy win. The family of the man who died had been told, three times, that their case was not worth pursuing. Then they called Aaron Maples.

What Happened

A worker was on a job site operating alongside heavy equipment when a Bobcat skid steer crushed him. He died at the scene. He left behind a family who would spend the next several months being told by attorneys that the circumstances of his death, while tragic, did not add up to a viable lawsuit.

The problem was not that someone had done nothing wrong. The problem was that figuring out exactly who had done what wrong, and proving it to the standard required in court, was going to be extraordinarily difficult. Multiple companies were involved on the job site. The equipment had been maintained by one entity, operated under the supervision of another, and the safety protocols had been set — or more accurately, not set — by a third. The chain of responsibility was tangled, and every defendant had the resources to hire the best defense lawyers in the state.

When a case like this lands on an attorney's desk, the calculation is straightforward: how much will it cost to investigate, retain experts, and litigate against well-funded defendants versus the likelihood of recovery? Three firms ran that math and decided the numbers did not work.

Why We Took It

Aaron Maples did not see an unwinnable case. He saw a case that required more work than most firms were willing to do.

The facts were complicated, but complicated is not the same thing as impossible. Complicated means you need better experts, deeper investigation, and a willingness to spend the time and money necessary to untangle the evidence. It means you have to be willing to go to war against defense teams that will fight every motion, challenge every expert, and drag the case out as long as possible hoping you will give up.

This is the kind of case Maples & Connick was built for. The firm's founding philosophy is simple: the cases other firms will not take are often the cases that matter most. When three lawyers have already said no, the family standing in your office is not shopping for representation — they are looking for their last chance at justice.

The Investigation

The first step was to reconstruct exactly what happened on that job site, minute by minute. Maples and his team pulled every available record: OSHA inspection reports, equipment maintenance logs, training certifications, corporate safety manuals, and the employment records of every person who had been on site that day.

What emerged was a picture of systemic failure. The Bobcat skid steer had known visibility limitations. The operator had received inadequate training for the specific conditions on that site. The general contractor had not implemented the safety protocols that industry standards required for a worksite where heavy equipment and personnel were operating in close proximity. And the corporate chain of command — the people who had the authority to require those protocols — had chosen speed and cost savings over their workers' lives.

The firm retained biomechanical engineers who could explain exactly how the forces involved in the accident caused the fatal injuries. Safety experts testified about what protocols should have been in place and were not. Accident reconstructionists built a detailed model of the sequence of events, showing that the death was not an unforeseeable accident but a predictable consequence of decisions made by people who should have known better.

The Fight

The defense did what well-funded defense teams always do. They filed motions. They challenged the qualifications of every expert. They argued that the worker bore responsibility for his own death. They produced their own experts who testified that the safety measures on the site met industry standards — a claim that crumbled under cross-examination when the firm's investigation revealed specific OSHA violations that had gone unaddressed.

Industrial accident litigation is a war of attrition. The defendants' strategy was clear: make the case so expensive and so exhausting that the plaintiff's attorney would accept a lowball settlement or drop the case entirely. It is a strategy that works against firms that do not have the resources or the resolve to stay in the fight.

Maples & Connick stayed in the fight.

The Resolution

Through aggressive litigation and meticulous preparation that made clear to the defendants what they would face at trial, the firm secured a $4.4 million settlement for the victim's family. It was vindication — not just financially, but for a family that had been told three times that the death of their loved one did not matter enough for any lawyer to take the risk.

The settlement was independently verified and is a matter of public record on Lawyers.com.

What This Case Represents

Every year, workers are killed on job sites across Louisiana, Alabama, and the Gulf South. Many of those families never pursue claims because the first attorney they consult tells them the case is too complicated or too risky. Those families deserve better than a quick assessment and a rejection letter.

Workplace injury and wrongful death cases involving heavy equipment, multiple contractors, and corporate defendants require a firm that has the financial resources to fund a complex investigation, the expert relationships to build a compelling technical case, and the trial experience to make the other side believe that you will take this all the way to a jury if necessary.

That is what happened here. Three firms said no. Maples & Connick said yes. And a family that had lost hope walked away with $4.4 million and the knowledge that someone had held the responsible parties accountable.
